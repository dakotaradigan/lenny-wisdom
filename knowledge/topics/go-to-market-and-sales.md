# Go-to-Market and Sales

## Overview

Go-to-market strategy is where product meets the real world -- and where most startups discover that a great product with a bad GTM motion loses to a good product with a great one. Across six guests who have built, led, and advised on GTM at companies ranging from pre-revenue startups to Stripe and Figma, a unifying insight emerges: your go-to-market motion is itself a product that must be designed, iterated, and optimized with the same rigor you apply to your actual product. The era of choosing a single channel and scaling it indefinitely is over.

April Dunford's positioning work underpins everything downstream. She has positioned over 200 products across a 25-year career and found the same pattern: when positioning is wrong, every downstream activity -- sales conversations, marketing campaigns, product roadmap priorities -- struggles. When positioning is right, everything clicks. Claire Butler demonstrates what product-led GTM looks like in practice at its best: Figma operated without a sales team for three years, growing entirely through individual contributor designers who loved the tool enough to champion it internally and stake their social capital on spreading it. Jeanne Grosser extends this by showing how to layer enterprise sales on top of PLG at Stripe without destroying the bottom-up magic, and introduces the emerging role of the GTM engineer -- a technical role automating repetitive sales workflows with AI.

On the distribution and channel side, Emily Kramer's Fuel and Engine framework eliminates the confusion about marketing roles and investments by reducing everything to two questions: do you have compelling content and messaging (fuel), and do you have a way to get it to the right people (engine)? Eli Schwartz brings the product-minded approach to SEO, arguing that product managers -- not marketing teams -- should own search strategy because it is fundamentally a question about what users are looking for, not what keywords rank well. Jen Abel provides the founder-led sales playbook for the critical zero-to-one stage, where the founder IS the product, the actual product may be barely functional, and early sales conversations are about learning as fast as humanly possible rather than closing deals.

## Cross-Guest Synthesis

The six guests in this topic span the full GTM lifecycle: Dunford handles the foundational positioning that everything else depends on. Abel covers the zero-to-one founder-led sales stage. Butler and Schwartz address product-led acquisition channels (community-driven and search-driven respectively). Kramer provides the organizational blueprint for building marketing capability. Grosser shows how to evolve from PLG to enterprise at scale and how AI is reshaping the entire GTM function.

The critical insight that emerges from their synthesis is that GTM is not a department or a strategy -- it is an integrated system where each component constrains or amplifies the others. Poor positioning (Dunford) means even the best founder-led sales conversations (Abel) waste energy explaining the wrong value proposition. A great IC champion motion (Butler) fails if the product does not have enough self-serve value to create champions in the first place. Perfect content marketing (Kramer's fuel) goes nowhere without distribution (Kramer's engine). SEO investment (Schwartz) in the wrong keywords generates traffic that does not convert.

The guests also reveal a temporal pattern: what works at each stage of company growth is different. Founder-led sales (Abel) dominates from 0-10 customers. PLG and community-led growth (Butler) carry the company from 10-1000. Marketing engines and SEO (Kramer, Schwartz) add leverage from 100-10000. Enterprise sales and GTM engineering (Grosser) become essential at scale. Positioning (Dunford) must be revisited at every transition because the competitive landscape and customer composition shift.

## Key Patterns

1. **Positioning is the foundation, not an afterthought.**
April Dunford has repositioned hundreds of products and found the same pattern: when products are not selling, the problem is usually positioning, not the product itself. Repositioning the same product into a different market category -- without changing a single feature -- can dramatically transform results. Her methodology starts from competitive alternatives (what would customers use if you did not exist?) rather than from your own features, which is counterintuitive but produces dramatically better positioning. Every other GTM activity -- messaging, sales narrative, marketing campaigns, even product roadmap -- inherits the quality of your positioning work.

2. **Product-led and sales-led are not mutually exclusive -- they are sequential.**
Claire Butler shows Figma's bottom-up motion where IC champions organically spread the tool through organizations, with enterprise revenue following when companies need procurement to organize widespread usage. Jeanne Grosser shows how Stripe layered deeply consultative sales on top of self-serve, requiring salespeople who "pass for PMs" when talking to engineers. The pattern: start with whichever motion matches your product's natural adoption curve, then layer the other when the first plateaus. Trying to run both from day one dilutes focus.

3. **The founder is the product in early sales.**
Jen Abel's core insight: in the zero-to-one stage, the actual product may be abstract or MVP-stage, but the founder's novel insight and vision is what gets the market excited. Nobody else can detect the budding moments in early conversations that reveal where true product-market fit lives. Day one vision is never the same as the vision that achieves PMF, and that evolution happens through founder-led sales conversations. Interest rates on outreach are driven by the quality of the founder's insight, not by email technique -- identical methods can produce 2% vs 12% response rates depending on the insight.

4. **Marketing archetype must match business model.**
Emily Kramer's framework is definitive: a self-serve PLG product needs a growth marketer who understands funnels and experimentation. A sales-led enterprise product needs demand gen or field marketing that understands pipeline. A consumer product needs brand marketing. Hiring the wrong archetype for your model is the most common marketing hiring mistake, and defaulting to "marketing generalist" is almost never the right answer. The first marketing hire is one of the most consequential hiring decisions a startup makes.

5. **Distribution is harder than creation.**
Emily Kramer: content without a distribution engine is a car without wheels. A great engine with no fuel (running ads to a bad landing page) wastes money. Eli Schwartz: SEO that targets keywords nobody actually searches for is wasted effort -- step one, which almost everyone misses, is identifying what someone would actually search to find your product. Claire Butler: Figma's scrappy distribution tactics (sliding into a friend's ex-boyfriend's DMs after seeing they signed up) were as important as the product quality. The pattern: creating great work is necessary but insufficient; getting it to the right people requires its own deliberate strategy.

6. **Enterprise customers buy to avoid pain, not to gain upside.**
Jeanne Grosser's observation that 80% of customers buy to reduce risk or avoid pain rather than for increased upside fundamentally changes how you sell. Startups love talking about the art of the possible, but enterprises buy to avoid missing their revenue target next quarter. This disconnect between how startups pitch and how enterprises buy is one of the most common reasons early enterprise sales fail.

7. **AI is collapsing GTM specialization.**
Jeanne Grosser describes the GTM engineer role: a technical person who shadows top-performing salespeople, documents their workflows, then builds AI agents to automate the repetitive parts. At Vercel, one GTM engineer spent six weeks building an inbound lead agent that replaced 9 of 10 SDRs on inbound, maintaining the same lead-to-opportunity conversion rate while reducing response time. The hyper-specialization era of 17+ GTM roles (SDR, BDR, AE, CSM, SA, etc.) is collapsing back toward integrated, AI-augmented workflows. Sales process documentation is now a prerequisite for AI automation.

## Frameworks to Apply

- **Positioning Framework (April Dunford):**
Define positioning through five components in order: (1) Competitive alternatives -- what would customers use if you did not exist? (2) Unique attributes -- what can you do that alternatives cannot? (3) Differentiated value -- what value do those attributes enable? (4) Target customer segments -- who cares most about that value? (5) Market category -- what context makes your value obvious? Always start from competitive alternatives, never from your own features. Good positioning flows directly into sales narrative, marketing messaging, and product roadmap priorities.

- **Fuel and Engine (Emily Kramer):**
Diagnose your marketing by asking two questions: Is the problem fuel (content, messaging, copy, design that adds value) or engine (distribution channels, tracking, ops that get it to people)? Every company needs an engine. The strategic question is which side is your biggest bottleneck right now. Forget traditional org charts of product marketing, content, demand gen -- just think fuel and engine.

- **Bottom-Up IC Champion Model (Claire Butler):**
Win over individual contributors who love your product enough to stake their social capital on spreading it within their organizations. Use node graphs to track how usage spreads person-to-person. Enterprise revenue follows when companies organically need to organize widespread usage through procurement. At early stages, look for qualitative signals (people pulling the laptop from your hands) rather than trying to optimize conversion metrics on small numbers.

- **Go-to-Market as a Product (Jeanne Grosser):**
Map the entire customer lifecycle -- from awareness to high LTV -- as a product you design. Identify jobs to be done at each stage, orchestrate across functions, and iterate like you would with your actual product. The buying experience itself increasingly differentiates companies when products converge. Forward-deployed engineering (professional services rebranded) is genuine when learnings flow back to product to determine what is generalizable vs what remains a service.

- **Product-Led SEO (Eli Schwartz):**
Think of SEO as a product question: what will a user search for to discover this product? If you cannot answer with specificity, do not invest in SEO. In the AI Overviews era, the opportunity shifts from ranking on broad discovery keywords to owning specific, intent-rich mid-funnel queries. The user journey now starts with an AI-summarized answer that provides direction, then moves to mid-funnel search where SEO matters most.

## Socratic Questions

1. Can you articulate your positioning using April Dunford's five components -- starting from competitive alternatives rather than from your own features?

2. If you repositioned your product into a completely different market category, which category would make your differentiated value most obvious to your best customers?

3. Is your current GTM bottleneck fuel (content, messaging, creative) or engine (distribution, tracking, ops)? What specific evidence supports that diagnosis?

4. Who are the individual contributors inside your target organizations who would love your product enough to champion it internally? What would make them stake their professional reputation on recommending it?

5. If your founder stopped doing sales calls tomorrow, would the pipeline survive? If not, what specific insight or approach has not yet been systematized into a repeatable sales process?

6. Are you selling to avoid pain (what 80% of enterprise buyers actually want) or selling upside potential (what startups naturally pitch)? How would your sales narrative change if you led with risk reduction?

7. What would someone actually search for to discover your product? Can you answer with a specific query string, or is the answer vague? If vague, should you be investing in SEO at all?

8. Is your first marketing hire matched to your business model -- growth marketer for PLG, demand gen for sales-led, brand for consumer -- or are you defaulting to "marketing generalist" because you are unsure?

9. When was the last time you tested your sales pitch on someone with no context on your product and asked them to repeat back what you do and why it matters?

10. How would your GTM motion change if you treated the buying experience as a product with its own roadmap, metrics, and iteration cycles -- rather than as a process owned by multiple disconnected teams?

11. What does your sales process documentation look like? Could a GTM engineer shadow your best salesperson and automate the repetitive parts, or is the process still entirely in people's heads?

## GTM by Company Stage

The guests collectively reveal that GTM strategy must evolve with company stage:

**Pre-Revenue (0-10 customers):**
- The founder IS the product (Abel). Nobody else can detect where PMF lives.
- Use Jen Abel's four-component cold outreach: relevancy, counterintuitive hook, problem focus, concision.
- Be vulnerable on sales calls: "We're early-stage and learning. Can we gain your insight?"
- Interest rate is driven by founder insight quality, not email technique.
- Claire Butler's signal-over-metrics principle: look for qualitative pull, not conversion rates.

**Early Traction (10-100 customers):**
- Positioning becomes critical (Dunford). You may need to reposition based on what you learned in founder-led sales.
- Emily Kramer's first marketing hire decision: match the archetype to your business model.
- If PLG, start building IC champion motions (Butler). Track node graphs of person-to-person adoption.
- Eli Schwartz's question: what would someone search to find you? If you can answer it, SEO investment starts now.
- Document your sales process -- this is a prerequisite for everything that follows (Grosser).

**Growth (100-1000 customers):**
- Layer the second GTM motion: add sales to PLG, or PLG to sales-led (Grosser, Butler).
- Emily Kramer's fuel and engine diagnostic: identify which side is the bottleneck and invest accordingly.
- Invest in SEO as a compounding channel with long payback periods (Schwartz).
- Transition from founder-led to team-led sales, systematizing the insight (Abel).

**Scale (1000+ customers):**
- Jeanne Grosser's GTM engineering: automate repetitive sales workflows with AI agents.
- Revisit positioning as competitive landscape shifts (Dunford).
- Build forward-deployed engineering for enterprise accounts, flowing learnings back to product (Grosser).
- Enterprise sales teams must pass the "10 engineers can't tell you're not a PM" test (Grosser).

## Common Pitfalls

1. **Positioning by committee or by copying competitors.**
April Dunford finds that most companies guess at positioning or copy what competitors say. Starting from competitive alternatives -- what customers would actually use if you did not exist -- rather than internal assumptions is counterintuitive but produces dramatically better positioning. Most products can be positioned in multiple market categories; the right one makes your value obvious.

2. **Hiring the wrong marketing archetype for your business model.**
Emily Kramer's most common diagnosis: the first marketing hire is mismatched to the business model. A growth marketer at a sales-led enterprise company flounder, and a demand gen marketer at a PLG company waste resources on channels that do not match the adoption motion. Identify your model first, then hire the archetype.

3. **Trying to optimize your way to product-market fit.**
Claire Butler: "You can't optimize your way to product-market fit." At early stages, small numbers make metrics unreliable. Look for qualitative signals -- are people pulling the laptop from your hands to try the product? -- not conversion rate optimization on a sample size of 50.

4. **Outsourcing early sales to a salesperson before the founder has sold.**
Jen Abel insists the founder must do early sales personally because nobody else can detect the budding moments that reveal where PMF lives. Hiring a sales rep before the founder has closed deals means losing the most valuable learning channel in the business. Founder-led sales is about learning, not revenue.

5. **Building fuel with no engine.**
Emily Kramer: creating great content without a distribution mechanism is like building a car with no wheels. Many startups invest heavily in content marketing -- blog posts, whitepapers, case studies -- while neglecting how that content reaches the right people. Conversely, running a great engine with no fuel (ads to a bad landing page) burns money.

6. **Targeting broad discovery keywords in the AI era.**
Eli Schwartz warns that AI Overviews are swallowing the top-of-funnel discovery layer of search, appearing on most search results and expanding internationally. The opportunity has shifted to mid-funnel, intent-rich queries where users have specific needs. Companies still optimizing for broad discovery keywords are fighting the last war.

7. **Hiring transactional salespeople for technical products.**
Jeanne Grosser's litmus test: "If I put you in front of 10 engineers at our company, it should take them 10 minutes to figure out you aren't a product manager." Sales teams for technical products must be deeply consultative. Transactional sales techniques that work for commodity products fail catastrophically when the buyer is technical and the product is complex.
