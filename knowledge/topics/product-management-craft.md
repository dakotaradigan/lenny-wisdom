# Product Management Craft

## Overview

Product management craft is the topic where Lenny's guests are most passionately divided -- and that division reveals the field's most important tension. Marty Cagan draws a sharp line between "empowered product teams" that receive problems to solve and "feature teams" that receive roadmaps to execute, arguing that only 10-15% of companies operate with true empowerment. Shreyas Doshi pushes PMs to think at the impact level rather than getting trapped in execution details. Meanwhile, Naomi Gleit demonstrates that extreme operational clarity -- canonical documents, numbered lists, traffic light decision matrices -- is itself a form of craft that enables everything else.

The synthesis across guests is that great product management operates simultaneously at three levels: discovery (understanding what to build), execution (getting it built and shipped), and influence (creating organizational alignment and momentum). Teresa Torres provides the scaffolding for discovery through Opportunity Solution Trees and continuous customer interviews. Shreyas Doshi provides the prioritization lens through LNO and opportunity cost thinking. Naomi Gleit and Jackie Bavaro provide the execution and influence toolkit. Ravi Mehta maps all of this into a structured competency model that helps PMs identify their specific gaps.

What unites every guest is the belief that product sense is not innate. Ravi Mehta describes it as pattern recognition built through deliberate practice. Jackie Bavaro calls strategy a learnable skill. Teresa Torres insists that discovery is a set of habits, not a talent. Marty Cagan's critique of feature teams is not that the PMs are inadequate -- it is that the organizational model prevents them from doing real product work. The consistent message is that the craft is accessible to anyone willing to do the work, but that the work is harder and more multidimensional than most people realize.

## Key Patterns

- **Most execution problems are actually strategy or culture problems.** Shreyas Doshi observes that in high-performing organizations, when the same issue keeps recurring despite bandaids, the root cause is almost always strategic ambiguity or cultural dysfunction, not execution failure. Naomi Gleit adds that 80% of project failures stem from people or process issues rather than strategy.

- **Discovery and delivery must run in parallel, not sequentially.** Teresa Torres's continuous discovery framework rejects the notion that you first do research, then build. Everything in the backlog is a bet; discovery helps you make better bets. One customer interview per week is sufficient to start.

- **The highest-leverage PM work is the work you are procrastinating on.** Shreyas Doshi's LNO framework identifies that PMs instinctively avoid Leverage tasks -- the ambiguous, high-stakes work that creates 10-100X returns -- in favor of Neutral and Overhead tasks that feel productive but are not transformative.

- **Say yes to the problem, control the solution.** Jackie Bavaro's career-changing insight at Asana was learning to say yes to the problem someone raises while maintaining ownership of the solution. This unlocks genuine collaboration instead of defensive roadmap protection.

- **Create clarity where there is ambiguity.** Ravi Mehta identifies this as the meta-skill of great PMs, requiring both analytical rigor and the ability to synthesize messy information into crisp narratives. Naomi Gleit operationalizes this through canonical documents, numbered lists, and traffic light matrices.

- **The product trio is the unit of effectiveness.** Teresa Torres, Marty Cagan, and Camille Fournier all converge on the principle that PM, designer, and engineer must discover and decide together. When trios work from shared understanding, disagreements drop dramatically and solutions improve.

- **Product sense develops through deliberate decomposition.** Ravi Mehta describes building product sense by decomposing products into their component decisions, understanding the trade-offs behind each, and developing pattern recognition across many products. It is a skill built through practice, not a gift.

## Frameworks to Apply

- **Opportunity Solution Tree** — Use when your team generates lots of ideas but struggles to connect them to real user problems. Map desired outcomes at the top, branch into opportunities, then solutions and experiments. The key discipline: 98% of people write solutions when they think they are writing opportunities.

- **LNO Framework** — Use when overwhelmed with tasks and needing to ruthlessly prioritize your time. Categorize everything as Leverage (apply perfectionism), Neutral (do adequately), or Overhead (minimize effort). The same task can be L, N, or O depending on context.

- **Pre-mortem** — Use before any major launch or commitment. Imagine the project has failed, work backwards to identify Tigers (real threats), Paper Tigers (seeming threats), and Elephants (unspoken issues). Creates psychological safety for raising risks.

- **PM Competency Model** — Use for career development and identifying specific skill gaps. Ravi Mehta's framework maps 12 competencies across Product Execution, Customer Insight, Product Strategy, and Influencing People, with clear level expectations from IC to VP.

- **Continuous Discovery Habits** — Use when you need to build a sustainable practice of talking to customers. Automate recruiting, conduct one interview per week, collect specific stories about recent experiences rather than asking hypothetical questions.

## Socratic Questions

1. Are you operating as an empowered product team that receives problems to solve, or a feature team that receives a roadmap? What would need to change in your organization for you to have more empowerment?

2. What is your highest-leverage task right now -- the one that would create 10X returns? Is it also the task you are most likely to procrastinate on?

3. When was the last time you talked to a customer? Not a sales call or a support ticket -- a genuine discovery conversation exploring their experience?

4. Can you draw your Opportunity Solution Tree right now? Does every solution on your roadmap trace back to a specific, well-understood user opportunity?

5. How do you distinguish between a strategy problem and an execution problem on your team? What signals would tell you the real constraint is strategic ambiguity rather than execution speed?

6. If someone brings you a feature request, do you instinctively say no to protect the roadmap, or do you explore the underlying problem they are trying to solve?

7. What are the top three risks to your current project? Have you explicitly named them and categorized them as Tigers, Paper Tigers, or Elephants?

8. Using Ravi Mehta's PM Competency Model, where are your specific gaps? Are you strongest in Product Execution, Customer Insight, Product Strategy, or Influencing People?

9. Is your team comparing multiple solutions for each opportunity, or defaulting to the first idea that seems reasonable?

10. How much of your time do you spend on Overhead tasks that could be minimized? What would your week look like if you protected more time for Leverage work?

11. Do your engineers feel creatively included in product decisions? When was the last time an engineer on your team originated a product idea that shipped?

## Common Pitfalls

- **Operating as a feature team while believing you are empowered.** Marty Cagan estimates only 10-15% of companies have truly empowered product teams. The test: do you receive problems to solve, or solutions to build? If you receive a roadmap, you are a feature team regardless of your title.

- **Spending too much time validating known problems instead of discovering solutions.** Marty Cagan argues that founders usually know the problem. PMs should spend minimal time validating obvious problems and maximum time crafting solutions that customers will actually choose over alternatives.

- **Chasing ROI instead of minimizing opportunity cost.** Shreyas Doshi warns that ROI thinking biases toward quick wins and low-hanging fruit. Opportunity cost thinking asks "is this the best use of my time?" rather than "is this a good use of my time?"

- **Writing solutions when you think you are writing opportunities.** Teresa Torres identifies this as the most common mistake in discovery work. An opportunity is an unmet need, pain point, or desire -- not a feature idea.

- **Treating product management as project management.** When PMs spend their days herding cats, writing tickets, and managing timelines rather than discovering and validating solutions, the organization has a structural problem, not a people problem.

- **Ignoring optics.** Shreyas Doshi's Three Levels of Product Work reminds PMs that Impact and Execution matter, but Optics -- creating awareness of your work's impact -- is also essential. Leaders who do not see your impact cannot advocate for you.

- **Mediating between engineers and stakeholders instead of connecting them directly.** Camille Fournier identifies "playing telephone" as one of the four things that most annoys engineers about PMs. Connect people directly rather than being a communication bottleneck.
