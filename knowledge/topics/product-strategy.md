# Product Strategy

## Overview

Product strategy is the connective tissue between a company's vision and its day-to-day execution. Across Lenny's podcast, strategy guests converge on a counterintuitive insight: most companies do not actually have a strategy. They have goals, aspirations, or a long list of initiatives -- but none of these qualify as strategy. Richard Rumelt defines strategy as "a design for overcoming a high-stakes challenge," requiring a clear diagnosis, a guiding policy, and coherent actions. When any element is missing, what remains is what he calls "bad strategy" -- fluff, goals masquerading as plans, or incoherent actions that work against each other.

The guests approach strategy from complementary angles. Gibson Biddle provides the product-level lens (delight customers in hard-to-copy, margin-enhancing ways), Hamilton Helmer provides the business-level lens (achieve durable competitive advantage through one of seven powers), and April Dunford provides the market-level lens (position your product so its value is obvious to the right customers in the right category). Geoffrey Moore adds the temporal dimension: the right strategy depends entirely on where you sit in the technology adoption lifecycle, and using the wrong playbook for your phase will fail even with perfect execution.

A unifying theme is that strategy is demystifiable and learnable. Chandra Janakiraman has developed a repeatable 8-12 week process for creating product strategy that he has battle-tested across five companies. His core message -- that strategy does not require an innate "strategy gene" -- resonates with Jackie Bavaro's assertion that strategy is a concrete set of skills, not a mystical trait. The best strategies emerge from collaborative processes, not solitary genius.

## Key Patterns

- **Strategy requires focus, and focus means saying no.** Richard Rumelt insists that having 17 priorities means you have zero priorities. Gibson Biddle killed advertising and DVD features at Netflix to focus maniacally on personalization. Geoffrey Moore's beachhead strategy demands concentrating on one small segment until it catches fire rather than spreading thin.

- **Good strategy has a source of power -- some asymmetry that gives you an edge.** Hamilton Helmer's Seven Powers framework identifies specific economic structures (scale economies, network effects, counter-positioning, switching costs, branding, cornered resource, process power) that create durable advantage. Without at least one, strategy is just a wish.

- **Positioning is foundational, not cosmetic.** April Dunford shows that when positioning is wrong, everything downstream -- sales, marketing, product direction -- struggles. Repositioning the same product into a different market category can dramatically change results without changing a single feature. Start from competitive alternatives, not from your own features.

- **Strategy is a hypothesis to test, not a plan to execute.** Gibson Biddle treated Netflix product strategies as high-level hypotheses: roughly 6 out of 10 failed, but the 3-4 that succeeded created enormous value. The implication is that strategy requires generating many hypotheses and testing rigorously, not committing to a single bet.

- **The right go-to-market playbook depends on your adoption phase.** Geoffrey Moore's technology adoption lifecycle shows that visionaries, pragmatists, and mainstream buyers require fundamentally different approaches. Using the chasm-crossing playbook during the tornado phase, or vice versa, leads to failure regardless of execution quality.

- **Collaborative strategy creation builds buy-in that solo strategy never can.** Chandra Janakiraman's strategy working group -- engineering, product, design, and data collectively generating problems, clustering, and ranking -- produces strategies that people accept because the ideas came from them. The debate itself is the alignment mechanism.

- **Operational excellence is not strategy.** Hamilton Helmer's "treadmill" metaphor is stark: best team, fastest execution, most intense effort can all be mimicked. Power is what remains when competitors try their hardest to catch up. Companies that confuse operational execution with strategic advantage are running without building anything durable.

## Frameworks to Apply

- **DHM Model** — Use when evaluating strategic initiatives. Ask whether each initiative is Delightful for customers, Hard-to-copy by competitors, and Margin-enhancing for the business. All three must be true. Filters out ideas that are delightful but easy to replicate.

- **Seven Powers** — Use when evaluating long-term defensibility. Identify which of the seven powers your business can achieve, and recognize that different powers become available at different stages. Even pre-PMF, ask which business propositions tilt toward the availability of power.

- **Positioning Framework** — Use when your product is misunderstood, when entering a new market, or when the competitive landscape shifts. Start from competitive alternatives and work through unique attributes, differentiated value, target segments, and market category.

- **The Kernel of Strategy** — Use when stress-testing whether you actually have a strategy. Every good strategy requires a diagnosis (what is going on), a guiding policy (how to deal with it), and coherent actions (what to do). If any element is missing, you do not have a strategy.

- **Product Strategy Playbook** — Use when you need to develop or refresh product strategy for a team or business unit. Chandra's 8-12 week process provides a structured, repeatable approach that prevents strategy from being a mystical exercise.

## Socratic Questions

1. Can you articulate your product strategy as a diagnosis, a guiding policy, and a set of coherent actions? If you can only state goals or aspirations, you may not have a strategy yet.

2. What is your source of power? Which of the Seven Powers does your business have or is building toward? If you cannot name one, what does that imply about your long-term defensibility?

3. If a competitor copied your product feature-for-feature tomorrow, what would remain that they could not replicate? Is that advantage growing or eroding over time?

4. How would your ideal customer describe the category your product belongs to? Does that match how you position it? What competitive alternatives are they actually comparing you against?

5. Where does your product sit in the technology adoption lifecycle? Are you selling to visionaries, crossing the chasm to pragmatists, or riding a tornado? Is your go-to-market playbook matched to that phase?

6. What are you explicitly choosing not to do? If your strategy does not have clear non-focuses, is it actually providing focus?

7. When was the last time you tested a strategic hypothesis that failed? If all your strategic bets succeed, are you being bold enough?

8. If you had to explain your strategy to a new intern on their first day, could they understand it? The newspaper headline test: what would the headline say about your product's impact in two years?

9. Are you confusing operational excellence with strategic advantage? Would your competitive position survive if your team's execution quality dropped to merely average?

10. Who in your organization was involved in creating the strategy? If it was developed by one person in isolation, how much genuine buy-in does it actually have?

## Common Pitfalls

- **Mistaking goals for strategy.** Richard Rumelt identifies this as the most common failure: "grow revenue 20%" is a goal, not a strategy. Strategy explains how you will overcome the specific challenges in your path.

- **Trying to do too many things.** The most common strategy failure is having too many priorities. Geoffrey Moore's bonfire analogy applies: running a match back and forth under a log will never light it. Concentrate resources on one beachhead.

- **Treating positioning as a marketing exercise.** April Dunford shows that positioning is the strategic foundation that determines everything downstream. When companies treat it as copywriting, they miss the deeper question of which market category makes their value obvious.

- **Conflating strategy with roadmap.** Chandra Janakiraman is explicit: a strategy document should not include a roadmap. Strategy sits between mission/vision and the plan. Including a roadmap conflates the two and undermines the strategy's shelf life.

- **Skipping leadership interviews before building strategy.** Chandra's "fruit story" illustrates the cost: presenting a strategy to leaders who were never consulted produces a cycle of rejection. Interview leadership before strategy creation to understand their constraints and preferences.

- **Assuming operational excellence is a moat.** Hamilton Helmer's treadmill concept warns that even the best execution can be mimicked. Without a structural source of power, you are running hard just to stay in place.

- **Using the wrong playbook for your adoption phase.** Geoffrey Moore warns that what works for visionaries fails with pragmatists, and what works in the chasm fails in the tornado. Diagnose your phase before choosing your approach.
