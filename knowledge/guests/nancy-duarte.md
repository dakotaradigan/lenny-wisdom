Author of multiple books on communication and storytelling. Her TED talk on the secret structure of great talks has shaped how a generation thinks about presentations.

## Key Frameworks

### What Is / What Could Be
Structure presentations as an alternating rhythm between the current state (what is) and the future possibility (what could be). This creates longing for the future in your audience. The pattern repeats -- what is, what could be, what is, what could be -- building tension and release like a story, ending with a "new bliss" that paints the transformed future state.

### Audience as Hero
The presenter is not the hero -- they are the mentor (like Obi-Wan Kenobi). The audience holds the real power because they choose whether to accept or reject your idea. The mentor provides both an inner tool (resolve, new perspective) and an outer tool (specific action or capability) to help the hero get unstuck.

### Three Bullets for Any Presentation
(1) Your audience is the hero -- start with empathy for their situation. (2) Infuse your talk with story structure -- the rise and fall of tension, not just information. (3) Ask yourself: can they see what I'm saying? -- make ideas visual and concrete.

## Core Ideas

- The what-is/what-could-be structure works in any format -- from keynotes to phone calls to getting your spouse to do weekend chores
- This same pattern appears in both Martin Luther King Jr's speeches and Steve Jobs's iPhone launch, confirming it as a universal story structure
- Putting presentation tools in the hands of the masses destroyed the medium; most slides should be visual and clear, not walls of text
- Before important internal presentations, do a listening tour with surveys and interviews, then create a rough cut and get feedback from the next level of leaders before the final delivery
