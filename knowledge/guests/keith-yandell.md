# Keith Yandell

Longtime leader at DoorDash (~7 years) who has led legal, HR, marketing, customer support, BD, and corporate development teams. Previously led litigation at Uber. Known for extreme versatility and empathetic, direct leadership style. The culture he helped build at DoorDash emphasizes customer obsession, humility, and a strict no-politics, no-assholes standard.

## Key Frameworks

### The Asshole Test
When a candidate comes across poorly in an interview, give them direct feedback and see how they respond. Keith told a VP of Engineering candidate "you seemed like an asshole" over dinner. The candidate's genuine, humble response -- wanting to understand what he did wrong regardless of the hiring outcome -- became the reason he joined. Willingness to enforce culture standards even for highly qualified candidates signals authentic values.

### WeDash (Customer Obsession Program)
All DoorDash employees are required to do deliveries four times per year. This is not symbolic -- it consistently surfaces product bugs (like routing by straight line instead of road), generates empathy for drivers and restaurants, and creates direct feedback loops. Keith does it monthly, often with his daughters, and every session produces actionable product insights.

### Founder-Shaped Culture
Companies take on the personality of their founders. Tony Xu's humility, competitiveness, and bias to action define DoorDash: cheap champagne in plastic flutes for the Series D celebration, the CEO doing customer support daily, and a willingness to stay up all night preparing champagne flutes so the smart people can focus on communications strategy.

## Core Ideas

- DoorDash nearly went out of business before their Series D -- they had weeks of runway and had been rejected by everyone; Tony's drive to keep going exemplifies that founders must find a way because it only takes one yes
- The best cultural indicators come from what the company does when no one is watching, not what is written on the wall
- Direct, empathetic feedback during hiring reveals how candidates handle vulnerability -- those who welcome it are the ones worth hiring
- Customer obsession is not a poster; it is a practice requiring regular, structured exposure to the actual customer experience
