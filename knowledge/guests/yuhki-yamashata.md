# Yuhki Yamashita

Chief Product Officer at Figma. Previously at Uber (both product leadership and Head of Design for New Mobility), YouTube, and Microsoft (PM on Hotmail and Windows 8). Unique background straddling product and design -- one of few product leaders who has managed design teams directly. Drawn to Figma's mission of blurring the boundary between design and product.

## Key Frameworks

### Collaborative Transparency as Product Philosophy
Figma's core insight is that making design visible and accessible to everyone (not just designers) changes how teams work. The early reaction "if this is the future of design, I'm quitting" was signal of a revolution, not a problem. When your product equips customers to champion a new way of working, they evangelize the movement, not just the tool.

### PM-Design Empathy Building
PMs should consider spending time managing design teams (or vice versa) to build empathy for each other's perspectives. As a PM, you facilitate trade-offs across business, engineering, and design. As a design leader, you must suspend feasibility concerns to push on the best possible experience. Both perspectives make you better at the other role.

### Pulse Check Alignment
During product reviews, Figma uses quick pulse checks to gauge team alignment in real time. Drop in a simple aligned/not-aligned check to surface disagreements before they compound. Fast, lightweight mechanisms for surfacing misalignment prevent costly downstream surprises.

## Core Ideas

- Product management was initially just a way to get exposure to all functions before choosing one; the interdisciplinary nature ended up being the destination, not a waypoint
- Watching how Figma spread within Uber during a cultural transformation toward transparency and inclusion was what drew Yuhki to the company
- Product-led growth requires deep understanding of how your product spreads organically; Figma's virality comes from the collaborative, multiplayer nature of the tool itself
- The best product teams at Figma operate in a fast-moving environment where speed and iteration are prioritized, drawing from Uber-era product philosophy
- Hiring for product teams should prioritize genuine curiosity and the ability to push on product vision from multiple angles, not just traditional PM skills
