# Jeffrey Pfeffer

Professor of Organizational Behavior at Stanford GSB, teaching one of the two most popular courses in the MBA program: Paths to Power. Author of 7 Rules of Power and multiple books on organizational dynamics. Has trained NFL executives, venture capitalists, and thousands of business leaders.

## Key Frameworks

### The Seven Rules of Power
1. Get out of your own way -- don't let discomfort with power prevent you from acquiring it. 2. Break the rules -- conventional paths produce conventional results. 3. Appear powerful -- physical presence, confidence, and how you show up matters. 4. Build a powerful brand -- no one promotes someone they don't know. 5. Network relentlessly. 6. Use your power -- power unused is power lost. 7. Success excuses almost everything -- once you've acquired power, how you got there will be forgiven or forgotten.

### The Knowing-Doing Gap
Knowledge without action is useless. Students must "Do Power" -- take a concrete action during the course to apply principles. One student's Doing Power project was to become head of economic policy for a presidential campaign. Six years out of business school, he was running the $6 trillion US budget.

### Suspend Judgment
If someone is on your critical path, the only relevant judgment is that they're on your critical path. Senior executives have mastered the skill of never revealing what they think of you -- because leaking disrespect destroys your ability to collaborate.

## Core Ideas

- These are skills, not personality traits. Political skill is associated with higher salary, faster promotion, greater career satisfaction, and less stress. Anyone can learn them.
- If you want power to be used for good, more good people need to have power. Refusing to engage with power dynamics doesn't make you virtuous -- it makes you ineffective.
- Nobody gets promoted if nobody knows who they are. Visibility without substance means people know you're useless. Substance without visibility means nobody knows your substance.
- The people who most need to learn power skills are those from backgrounds that put them at a structural disadvantage -- they can't afford to ignore how the world actually works.

## Notable Perspectives
