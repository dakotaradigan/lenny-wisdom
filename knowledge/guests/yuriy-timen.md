## Key Frameworks

### Give Channels an Appropriate Shot
The only thing worse than a channel not working is prematurely concluding it does not work because you did not give it a real test. When companies say "YouTube doesn't work for us," dig into what they actually tried -- often it was not designed to have a chance of succeeding from the start. Every channel deserves a well-structured, properly resourced test before being dismissed.

### Subscription Growth Levers
For subscription businesses, growth comes from optimizing across the full funnel: acquisition (where do users come from), activation (first value moment), conversion (free to paid), retention (staying subscribed), and expansion (upsell and cross-sell). Each lever requires different tactics and the sequencing depends on where the biggest bottleneck sits.

### Channel-Product Fit
Not every growth channel works for every product. The key is matching your product's natural strengths to channels where those strengths can be demonstrated. Grammarly's browser extension made content marketing and SEO natural fits because people already searching for writing help could immediately see value.

## Core Ideas

- Grammarly's nine-year growth journey demonstrates that sustained, compound growth comes from systematic experimentation across channels, not from a single viral moment
- The biggest mistake in growth is giving up on a channel after a bad first test that was poorly designed; iterate on the test design before abandoning the channel
- Consumer subscription growth requires patience; the payback periods are longer than B2B SaaS but the compounding effects are enormous
- Growth advisors can be most impactful when they help companies think systematically about their growth model rather than providing tactical channel-specific advice
- Freemium products need to balance giving enough value for free to drive adoption while clearly demonstrating the premium value that justifies conversion
