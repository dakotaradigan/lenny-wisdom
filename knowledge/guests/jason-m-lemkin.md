## Key Frameworks

### The "Would You Buy From Them?" Test
Your first two sales reps must be people you would buy your own product from. Not people with impressive LinkedIn logos, not people who can say ACV and NRR fluently -- people who, in a 30-minute conversation, explain your product and your customer's problems in a way you believe. This rep always works because you can trust them with your precious early leads.

### Two Reps Before a VP
Never hire a VP of Sales until you have two reps consistently hitting quota. The VP's job is to take you from rep 3 to 300 -- to scale a script that's starting to work. Hiring a VP before that is a Hail Mary that never works because you're asking one person to find product-market fit, be the first rep, be the second rep, and build a scalable process simultaneously.

### Founders as A+ Middlers
Even founders who hate sales are excellent at the middle of a sales conversation -- explaining the product, answering deep questions, demonstrating expertise. Customers love talking to CEOs. The hack: get good at the middle, then work up courage to ask "What would it take to get going next week?"

## Core Ideas

- Be honest about your sales motion. However your first 10-20 customers find you is your DNA. If they need conversations, demos, and onboarding, that's a sales-led motion -- don't flee from it because "sales is icky."
- Always hire two reps, not one, because you need to A/B test humans. With one rep, you can't tell if poor performance is the person or the market.
- Early sales reps must be product savants -- they need to go much deeper than 10 standard questions. Later, as you scale, reps can be more heterogeneous and lean on sales engineers.
- The biggest mistake: founders with $4M in funding hire a VP of Sales instead of doing the work themselves. That VP won't be there in 8 months, and $2M will be gone.
- In SaaS, you're not selling -- you're solving problems. Your job is to help the customer get into the "Tesla Model 3 Performance" today, not to sell a used car.
