# Ken Norton

Executive coach specializing in senior product leaders (CPOs, VPs of Product, directors). Spent 14 years at Google leading product teams that built Google Docs, Google Calendar, Google Maps, and worked at Google Ventures. Products he helped craft are used by over three billion people. Famous for the "Bring the Donuts" essay on servant leadership in product management.

## Key Frameworks

### Creative vs. Reactive Mindset
The shift from reactive (putting out fires, responding to requests, managing the backlog) to creative (proactively shaping a vision, imagining what could be) is the most important transition for product leaders. Most leaders spend the majority of their time reactive and need coaching to reclaim creative space.

### Art Over Science of Product Management
The science of PM (frameworks, metrics, processes) is important but overemphasized. The art -- intuition, judgment, imagination, storytelling, empathy -- is what separates good PMs from great ones. Advice from others is like cotton candy: nice sugar high but no lasting nutrition. Real growth comes from helping people find their own path.

### Coaching as Creative Partnership
Coaching is not mentoring or advice-giving. It is a partnership where the definition of success belongs to the client. The most important tools: listening, curiosity, intuition, and helping people see through their own limiting beliefs. If you fully understand a problem, you know the solution -- the coach's job is to help the client get to that understanding.

## Core Ideas

- Leadership starts from day one in product management; PMs lead without formal authority, and that is both the challenge and the opportunity
- Imposter syndrome is universal among ambitious product leaders; the most effective response is reframing discomfort as evidence of growth
- The most common PM blind spots emerge from over-reliance on what worked at a previous company without recognizing how context has changed
- "Bring the Donuts" was always a metaphor for servant leadership -- filling gaps, doing whatever needs to be done, regardless of title
