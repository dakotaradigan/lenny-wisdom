# Eric Simons

Co-founder and CEO of StackBlitz, the company behind Bolt -- the number-one web-based AI coding agent. StackBlitz spent seven years building WebContainer, an operating system that runs inside the browser. When AI arrived, this technology became the perfect foundation for Bolt, which went from zero to $40M ARR in about five months with a team of roughly 15-20 people. One of the fastest-growing products in startup history.

## Key Frameworks

### Overnight Success, Years in the Making
Bolt exemplifies the pattern of long technical investment suddenly finding explosive product-market fit. Seven years of building browser-based compute infrastructure seemed like a solution looking for a problem until AI coding made it the differentiating advantage: instant startup, local execution, and extreme reliability compared to cloud-based competitors.

### Technology-First, Then Problem Discovery
Counter to conventional wisdom, StackBlitz built the technology first and found the problem to solve later. The key judgment call for founders is knowing when to trust deep technical conviction even when the market application is not yet clear, and having the resilience to persist through years of uncertainty.

## Core Ideas

- Conviction in technical moats can be justified even when the product application is unclear, but it requires extraordinary founder patience and a small, loyal team
- Speed and reliability are the subtle but critical differentiators in AI coding tools; running compute in the browser locally using the user's CPU eliminates cloud latency and failure modes
- The free market is filled with rational actors; if people are paying at scale, the value proposition is real and the skeptics are wrong
- A core team of five to seven people who have worked together for five-plus years is rare and enormously valuable during hypergrowth
- When explosive growth starts, expect disbelief from investors and even yourself; the data may take months to convince everyone it is real
