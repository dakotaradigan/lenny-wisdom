# Melanie Perkins

CEO and co-founder of Canva ($42B+ valuation, $3.3B+ annual revenue, profitable for 8+ consecutive years). On track to be the most successful female tech founder in history. Started as a university student in Australia with no business or software experience. Rejected by 100+ investors before raising her first round.

## Key Frameworks

### Column B Thinking
Column A: look at the bricks around you and figure out what you can build. Column B: dream of the perfect castle on the hill, then work backward to build it. Most planning is Column A (stacking existing bricks). The breakthrough companies start from Column B -- the improbable, wildly ambitious future -- and then take microscopic steps toward it. The first step can be embarrassingly small as long as it moves in the right direction.

### Chaos to Clarity
Every idea starts on the chaos side. Add clarity one step at a time: write it down, create a pitch deck, refine designs, build a prototype. Each step makes the vision more real and visible to others. The early chaos stage is embarrassing because you have none of the answers, but that is exactly when to start.

### Crazy Big Goals with Celebrations
Set goals so ambitious you feel completely inadequate before them. Couple them with fun celebrations when achieved: smashing plates, releasing doves, La Tomatina festivals. Timing estimates are often wrong, but the goals themselves get achieved through persistent compounding effort over years.

## Core Ideas

- Investor rejection is feedback in disguise; each "no" generates a new slide in your pitch deck addressing the objection
- Break the mission into pillars (design anything, every ingredient, every language, every device) and take successive annual goals toward each
- Wild success and terrible failure exercises: spend time imagining both extremes for 10-year horizons to clarify what truly matters
- Systems break every time the company doubles in size; season openers and goal celebrations create rhythm amid constant change
- Canva spent two years rewriting their entire codebase (expected: six months) and could not ship any new features during that period -- persistence through that is what separates lasting companies
