# Nick Turley

Head of ChatGPT at OpenAI. Previously a product leader at Dropbox and Instacart. Helped conceive ChatGPT and took it from a hackathon prototype to 700M+ weekly active users and billions in revenue -- arguably the most successful consumer software product launch in history.

## Key Frameworks

### Maximally Accelerated
A forcing function question: "If this were the most important thing and you wanted to truly maximally accelerate it, what would you do?" Not every initiative needs this treatment, but asking the question distinguishes critical path work from nice-to-haves. Applied selectively alongside deliberate process where stakes demand it (e.g., safety).

### Ship to Learn (Empirical Product Development)
With AI products, you cannot reason about what users want a priori. The properties of your product are emergent and unknowable before shipping. Ship lean, watch what happens, then iterate on the use cases people actually pursue. ChatGPT itself was launched as a research demo expected to be wound down, then retained users unexpectedly.

### The Model Is the Product
There is no meaningful distinction between the AI model and the product. Improving the model on specific use cases (writing, coding, health) is product development. About one-third of retention improvement comes from model quality, one-third from new capabilities (search, memory, personalization), and one-third from traditional product work (removing login friction, UI improvements).

## Core Ideas

- ChatGPT emerged from a hackathon -- the team went from decision to ship in 10 days. Speed was not about competition but about learning. You will polish the wrong things if you polish before shipping in AI.
- Retention in ChatGPT follows a "smile curve" -- users try it, churn, then come back as they learn to delegate to AI. Delegating to an AI is an unnatural behavior that takes time to develop, which is why retention improves over cohort maturity.
- The $20/month pricing came from a Van Westendorp survey posted as a Google Form on Discord the night before launch. Many consequential decisions at OpenAI were made in minutes under extreme time pressure.
- Building AI products requires two modes: working backward from model capabilities (art -- what is the most awesome way to productize new tech?) and working forward from user needs (classic PM -- listen to customers, do data science, iterate). Both must coexist.
- Run towards risky use cases (health, relationships, life advice) rather than away from them. Disabling high-stakes use cases wastes the technology's potential to help people. The duty is to make those use cases excellent through careful model behavior work.
