# David Placek

Founder of Lexicon Branding, which pioneered the field of brand naming over four decades and 4,000+ projects. Created names including Pentium, PowerBook, BlackBerry, Swiffer, Impossible Burger, Sonos, Azure, Vercel, Windsurf, and CapCut. Employs 253 linguists across 76 countries and has invested millions in sound symbolism research.

## Key Frameworks

### Identify, Invent, Implement
Three-phase naming process: (1) Identify -- understand behavior (how you act now vs. future), map the competitive landscape for distinctiveness, create a creative framework. (2) Invent -- use three small teams of two (not large brainstorms), where only one team knows the real client and others work on disguised briefs, combined with a linguistic engineering layer using sound symbolism research. (3) Implement -- test, clear trademarks, and champion the name through client resistance.

### Asymmetric and Cumulative Advantage
A distinctive name gives you an advantage before you even launch. Over time, as people interact with the brand more, the bond between customer and name strengthens (cumulative advantage). Descriptive names like "Cloud Pro" never build this advantage because they disappear into a sea of similar names.

## Core Ideas

- You will not know the right name when you see it. Clients almost universally believe they will, and they almost universally do not. The best names feel uncomfortable at first -- "there is no power in comfort, not in the marketplace."
- Large brainstorming sessions produce worse names than small teams of two. Most winning names come from teams working on disguised briefs (thinking they are naming a bicycle instead of a cloud service) because freedom from the real constraints unlocks creativity.
- Every letter of the alphabet has measurable sound properties. S and Z are noisy letters (good for attention). Vowels create flow and calm. The balance of noise and flow in a name determines its energy and distinctiveness.
- "You don't want to make a statement. You want to start a story." Azure starts a story about possibility; Cloud Pro makes a statement about function.
