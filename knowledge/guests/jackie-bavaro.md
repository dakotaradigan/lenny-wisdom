# Jackie Bavaro

Author of the best-selling books Cracking the PM Interview and Cracking the PM Career. First PM at Asana, later head of product management. Previously in Google's APM program and a PM at Microsoft.

## Key Frameworks

### Strategy as a Learnable Skill
Strategy is not a mystical trait -- it is a set of concrete skills. It includes understanding the competitive landscape, making hard trade-offs, defining what you will and won't do, and connecting product choices to business outcomes. Jackie's Cracking the PM Career book breaks this down systematically.

### The Power of Saying Yes
Early in her career at Asana, Jackie defaulted to saying no to protect the roadmap. Her boss confronted her: "You have to stop shutting me down." A coach challenged her to say yes to everything for two weeks. The insight: you can say yes to the problem someone raises while still controlling the solution. This unlocks real collaboration.

### Working Your Way Into the Room
At Asana, Jackie gradually got herself into the founders' planning committee by first asking her boss to bring back answers, then prepping questions for him, then offering to attend to save him time. Framing it as helping your manager is an effective, honest way to expand your scope.

## Core Ideas

- The PM career path doesn't require people management. At senior PM level in big tech, you're making as much as a doctor or lawyer. Understand the IC track is a legitimate, fulfilling path -- don't blindly pursue management because it seems like the only way to grow.
- Principal PM roles are rare because they require having director-level impact with a small team. Platform teams and partnerships are the most natural fit for this archetype.
- Being a manager is lonelier, less fun, and more painful than being an IC. You carry secrets, navigate conflicting loyalties, and lose the camaraderie of being "one of the team." Try it as a two-way door.
- Almost anyone can learn anything -- when people struggle, it's usually because something isn't clicking, not because they lack ability. This belief drove both her books: demystify what interviewers actually want.
