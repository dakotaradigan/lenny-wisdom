# Dr. Becky Kennedy

Clinical psychologist, author, and CEO of Good Inside, one of the most popular parenting platforms. Her principles about human behavior, originally developed for parent-child relationships, translate directly to workplace leadership because all humans -- whether one or forty-five years old -- need the same things and express frustration in similar ways when those needs are unmet.

## Key Frameworks

### The Power of Repair
Repair is the number one relationship strategy. Go back to a person after a moment you were not proud of, take responsibility for your part, acknowledge the impact, and describe what you would do differently. Secure attachment (in families and at work) is not defined by getting it right all the time -- it is defined by willingness to repair. Nothing reestablishes trust and cooperation like repair.

### Connect Before Correct
Connection forms a bridge between two people so they can act in the same interest. Without connection, directives feel like transactions. The mindset matters more than the words: be present with someone without an agenda, even for 30 seconds. People feel your intention, not just your intervention. Connection is not soft -- it is the foundation for cooperation and productivity.

### Good Inside (Separating Identity from Behavior)
Separate behavior from identity. Someone who is late a lot is not "lazy" -- they lack a skill they need to manage something happening internally. The quickest way to have an unproductive conversation is to lose sight of the fact that someone is good inside. This reframe unlocks curiosity over judgment and makes hard conversations productive.

## Core Ideas

- Boundaries are what you tell someone you will do, and they require the other person to do nothing. Making a request is not a boundary. "If you continue to yell in this meeting, I will leave" is a boundary. "Please stop yelling" is a request.
- Resilience over happiness should be the goal for teams. People who can say "this is hard and I can do hard things" are more valuable than people who need everything to feel comfortable.
- "Perfect is creepy." Only non-humans can be perfect. The human condition is wanting to do well and messing up repeatedly. Embracing this creates psychological safety for teams to take risks and learn.
- The Most Generous Interpretation: before reacting to someone's behavior, ask what the most generous possible interpretation of their actions could be. This prevents the spiral of negative assumptions that destroy working relationships.

## Notable Perspectives
