# Ravi Mehta

Former CPO at Tinder and product leader at Facebook and Tripadvisor. Now founder of Outpace, focused on PM coaching and career development. Created the widely-used PM Competency Model that maps the skills product managers need at each career level. Known for deeply systematic thinking about what makes PMs effective and how to develop product leadership skills.

## Key Frameworks

### PM Competency Model
A structured framework mapping 12 PM competencies across four dimensions: Product Execution (fluency with data, feature specification, product delivery), Customer Insight (user science, voice of customer, empathy), Product Strategy (business outcome ownership, product vision, roadmapping), and Influencing People (stakeholder management, team leadership, managing up). Each competency has clear level expectations from IC to VP, enabling PMs to identify specific gaps and build targeted development plans.

### Four PM Archetypes
PMs tend to cluster into archetypes based on their strengths: the Craftsperson (deep product execution), the Visionary (product strategy and bold bets), the Operator (scaling and optimization), and the Technologist (technical depth driving product decisions). Knowing your archetype helps you find the right role-company fit and develop complementary skills.

## Core Ideas

- Product sense is not innate -- it is built through deliberate practice of decomposing products into their component decisions, understanding the trade-offs behind each, and developing pattern recognition across many products
- The best PM career moves optimize for learning rate, not title or compensation; each role should stretch you into uncomfortable territory
- When hiring PMs, weight real-world case studies and reference checks far more heavily than abstract problem-solving interviews
- Great PMs create clarity where there is ambiguity, which requires both analytical rigor and the ability to synthesize messy information into crisp narratives

## Notable Perspectives

> "Product sense is really just pattern recognition built from studying how great products work and don't work."
