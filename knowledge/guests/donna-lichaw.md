# Donna Lichaw

Executive coach, speaker, and bestselling author of The Leader's Journey. Former product manager and designer who pivoted to coaching after discovering that storytelling workshops failed to address the real leadership challenges teams faced: conflict, self-doubt, and inability to connect. Works with founders, CEOs, and executive teams at companies like Google, Disney, and Microsoft.

## Key Frameworks

### Story-Driven Leadership (Inside-Out)
Start with yourself at the center, then extend outward to one-on-one relationships, then teams, then the business. The stories we tell ourselves (not the stories we tell others) are the most powerful forces shaping our leadership. Our brains cannot distinguish between true and false narratives, so you may as well leverage that to be the hero of your story rather than the victim.

### Superpower Discovery
Pull superpowers out of stories from your past and present, then transpose them to your future. Use a data-driven approach: gather feedback from colleagues (your "customers"), cross-reference with self-perception, and identify where the stories you tell yourself diverge from reality. Most self-limiting stories are not true when tested against actual data.

## Core Ideas

- The most common leadership problem is not lack of skill but stories leaders tell themselves: "I'm too nice," "nobody listens to me," "I'm an imposter." These narratives become self-fulfilling prophecies when left unchallenged.
- Imposter syndrome can be functional. Instead of trying to eliminate it, ask how it serves you. One founder discovered her imposter syndrome kicked in at growth edges and drove her to read 20 books and take courses, making her better. It was a signal, not a bug.
- User-centered leadership (focusing on who you are bringing along) does not work if you have not first figured out your own mission, purpose, and fuel. The inside-out approach is not selfish -- it is the only sustainable model for leadership.
- When feedback reveals something is actually true (not just a false self-narrative), the solution is often counterintuitive. A quiet leader who was told she needed to "speak up more" actually needed to communicate her processing style, not force herself to talk more. The real problem was her team not knowing she was listening.
