# Jason Feifer

Editor in Chief at Entrepreneur Magazine. Previously an editor at Fast Company and other publications. Author, podcast host, keynote speaker, and startup advisor. Receives 30-50 pitches per recording session and deeply understands what makes media pay attention.

## Key Frameworks

### The Three-Step Press Process
Step 1 (Prep): Define what press is for -- awareness, investor interest, positioning, new market entry. Only pursue press when you have a tactical answer. Step 2 (Who to Pitch): Match your story fragments to specific publications based on their mission, not their name. Step 3 (The Pitch): Understand the publication's audience, craft a story that serves them, and reach out to the right person.

### Understand the Publication's Mission
Every publication has a mission that guides editorial decisions. Entrepreneur is about how to think through business challenges (not just "about entrepreneurs"). Fast Company was about where business is going. The New York Times Sunday Business is different from Monday-Saturday Business. Read the content, find the pattern, and pitch to the pattern.

### Editors Don't Care About You
Media serves their audience, not you. You're not ordering a hamburger from "Entrepreneur Hamburgers." Approach journalists with an understanding of what they're trying to do for their readers and how you can be useful in that. The butter dish story: the product isn't interesting, but the founder doing market research by surveying people at airport gates is.

## Core Ideas

- 75% of pitches come from PR; only 20-25% come directly from founders. Founder pitches stand out because they carry more weight and authenticity.
- Think about press the way you think about fundraising: don't pursue it unless you know what it's for. "I deserve coverage" is not a tactical reason.
- Match your story to the right publication. A DC hot dog truck pitching Entrepreneur Magazine is wasted effort -- they need local food press to drive hot dog sales.
- Freelance writers are often more likely to write about you than staff writers, because freelancers need story ideas to pitch editors.

## Notable Perspectives

> "The editor, the writer -- they don't care about you. They care about their reader or their listener or their viewer."

> "You don't reach out to an investor if you don't understand what kinds of companies they invest in. You can't do that with media, either."
