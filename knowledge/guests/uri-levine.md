# Uri Levine

Co-founder of Waze (sold to Google for $1.1B) and nine other companies. Two-time unicorn builder. Board member of 20 companies, advisor to 50+ startups. Author of Fall in Love with the Problem, Not the Solution. Serial entrepreneur whose core philosophy is that value creation starts with solving a problem people care about.

## Key Frameworks

### Fall in Love with the Problem
The problem serves as the North Star for the entire entrepreneurial journey. When you tell customers the problem you solve rather than what your system does, they want you to succeed and will help you get there. Start every pitch with "the problem we solve is" or "the value we create for you is" -- never with "our company is" or "our AI system is."

### The Three Dimensions of the Startup Journey
1) Roller coaster -- the frequency of ups and downs is dramatically higher than in established businesses. 2) Journey of failures -- you try things that do not work until you find one that does; if you are afraid to fail, you have already failed. 3) Long journey -- much longer than you expect, especially the period before product-market fit.

### 30-Day Hiring Test
After every new hire, mark your calendar for 30 days. Ask: knowing what I know today, would I hire this person? If the answer is no, fire them immediately. The cost of a bad hire compounds rapidly in a startup.

## Core Ideas

- Validate your problem by talking to 20+ strangers (not friends and family); if they rephrase the problem in their own words with passion, it is real; if they say "I know someone who has this problem," it is not
- Fail fast so you have time for more attempts; the more shots you take, the more likely one goes in; the biggest enemy of good enough is perfect
- Start your investor pitch with your strongest point on slide one (it is displayed the longest) and your second strongest on the last slide; the middle is least important
- B2C ideas sometimes need to pivot to B2B when consumers care about the problem but will not take action; Pontera shifted from consumer airfare monitoring to corporate travel savings
- The difference between a startup and a corporate is that a startup does not know its value proposition, pricing, target audience, or go-to-market -- and must figure all of them out
