# Gergely Orosz

Author of The Pragmatic Engineer, the number-one technology newsletter on Substack with nearly 200,000 subscribers. Former engineering manager at Uber Amsterdam, where he built payment systems and managed teams of 30+. Previously at Skyscanner, Microsoft/Skype, and various consulting companies. Left a $330K/year tech career to write, and now earns more from the newsletter than he did at Uber.

## Key Frameworks

### The Paid Newsletter Model
No ads or sponsorships -- revenue comes entirely from subscriptions. Free articles attract the audience; paid subscribers get more frequent, deeper content. Track the revenue impact of each article to understand what resonates. The model creates alignment: quality writing directly translates to subscription growth, giving yourself a raise with every great article.

### Career Risk as Compound Investment
Make a promise to yourself: if a financial milestone is reached, take a risk. Gergely promised himself that if Uber's stock vested successfully, he would try something new. The combination of financial cushion, COVID disruption, and dissatisfaction with the trajectory created the trigger. The key insight: you do not plan these transitions perfectly; you create conditions where you can afford to try.

## Core Ideas

- The transition from corporate tech to independent creator feels like going from one boss to thousands of micro-bosses; any one can fire you, but the aggregate is safer
- Growth can be surprisingly explosive when platforms add recommendation features; Substack recommendations grew the newsletter from 50K to 180K+ in months
- The life of a newsletter writer is deceptively demanding; consistency and volume are the table stakes for building a sustainable audience
- European engineers systematically undervalue stock compensation because they have fewer success-story reference points
- Starting a blog anonymously (as Gergely did) can be a low-risk way to test whether writing resonates before committing to it full-time

## Notable Perspectives
