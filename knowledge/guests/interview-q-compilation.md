# Interview Question Compilation

This is not a single guest episode -- it is a compilation of favorite interview questions from 17 different Lenny's Podcast guests, selected from over 100 episodes. Featured contributors include Eeke De Miliano (Retool/Stripe), Geoff Charles (Ramp), Shishir Mehrotra (Coda), Yuhki Yamashita (Figma), Katie Dill (Stripe), Karri Saarinen (Linear), Noah Weiss (Slack), Ben Williams (Snyk), Nikhyl Singhal (Facebook), Scott Belsky (Adobe), Lauryn Isford (Notion), and Paul Adams (Intercom).

## Key Frameworks

### The Eigenquestion Test (Shishir Mehrotra)
Present an open-ended scenario (e.g., "Scientists invented a teleportation device -- bring it to market"). Let candidates generate many questions, then constrain to only two. The best candidates quickly find the one or two questions whose answers determine everything else. Tests for ability to simplify and find leverage in ambiguity.

### The Controversial Decision Question (Yuhki Yamashita)
"Describe a time you were part of a controversial product decision." Reveals whether candidates can represent multiple perspectives even-handedly, understand why conflict existed, and demonstrate storytelling and communication ability.

### The Reference Call Hack (Paul Adams)
When doing reference calls, ask: "What feedback will I be giving this person in their first performance review?" The referee cannot dodge it -- there is always an answer, and it surfaces real growth areas.

## Core Ideas

- The best interview questions test for self-awareness, not rehearsed answers. Questions like "To what do you attribute your success? (And you can't say luck)" or "What's something everyone takes for granted that you think is inaccurate?" break candidates out of interview mode.
- "What's the hardest thing you've ever done?" reveals what difficulty means to a person, how they overcome it, and how much agency they demonstrated. Useful for high-intensity environments.
- "Tell me what work you are most proud of" -- asked by multiple guests (Katie Dill, Karri Saarinen, Camille Hearst) -- reveals taste, judgment, motivation, and what someone considers a good outcome.
- Questions about introspection consistently surface the best hires: "Tell me something you did that worked, but not for the reason you thought" or "What have you learned about yourself that reveals a limitation?"
