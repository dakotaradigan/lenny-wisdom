# Dalton Caldwell

Managing Director and Group Partner at Y Combinator, where he has worked across 21 batches and closely with the earliest days of Instacart, Retool, Brex, DoorDash, Webflow, Amplitude, and 20+ other unicorns. Former co-founder of imeem and App.net. Has seen more startups than nearly any human alive.

## Key Frameworks

### The Good Pivot
A successful pivot gets warmer, not colder, from your existing expertise. It builds on what you learned from the prior idea. Brex pivoted from VR headsets to fintech because the founders had fintech experience in Brazil. Retool pivoted from a Venmo competitor to internal tools because they had built dashboards for their own product. A good pivot is like going home.

### Tarpit Ideas
Ideas that seem like unsolved problems, attract many founders, and generate positive validation -- but consistently fail. The defining characteristic is that you CAN validate them. Apps to coordinate going out with friends, music discovery, Foursquare clones -- these are tarpits because they seem great and people have been starting them since the 1990s.

### Information Diet Diversification
If you consume the same podcasts, tweets, and blog posts as every other founder, you will have the same startup ideas. Go off the beaten path -- mine your personal experience, look at hated incumbents with low NPS, explore unfashionable spaces before they become fashionable.

## Core Ideas

- The most transformative advice is often the simplest: just don't die, sell things, make money. Like a basketball coach reminding players of fundamentals during a huddle, founders need constant affirmation that continuing to do high-quality reps is the game.
- The worst-looking companies in a YC batch are often the most successful. Brex and Retool were both considered the weakest companies in their batch before pivoting to become decacorns.
- When to keep going: you still love your customers, your product, and working with your co-founder. When to stop: it is profoundly affecting your wellbeing and relationships. Nobody will remember you shut down in 10-20 years if you handle it with integrity.
- When to pivot: you have run out of ideas for how to make it grow. If you still have a dozen good growth ideas untried, keep going.
