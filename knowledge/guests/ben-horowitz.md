# Ben Horowitz

Co-founder of Andreessen Horowitz (a16z), the world's largest venture capital firm with $46B+ in committed capital, and author of two NYT bestsellers: *The Hard Thing About Hard Things* and *What You Do Is Who You Are*. Previously CEO of Opsware/LoudCloud through an IPO with just $2M in trailing revenue.

## Key Frameworks

### Run Toward Fear
The worst thing a leader can do is hesitate when facing two terrible options. The psychological muscle of great leadership is looking into the abyss and choosing the slightly better path, even when both are horrible. Hesitation locks up the company and creates a political vacuum.

### Managerial Leverage
As CEO, you shouldn't be developing people into roles you don't understand yourself. If you're constantly telling reports what to do rather than them bringing ideas to you, you're getting no leverage. The sign to make a change is when you're losing leverage.

### Success as a Series of Small Decisions
Success isn't one brilliant move but a chain of small, hard decisions. Like a plane crash results from 17 bad decisions in a row, success comes from breaking psychological sunk costs and making each next small decision well.

## Core Ideas

- The only value a leader adds is when they make a decision most people don't like; if everyone agrees, they would have done it without you
- Founders fail primarily because they lose confidence, which causes hesitation, which creates political dysfunction
- Judge people by their strengths, not their worst moment; invest in strength, not lack of weakness
- The PM role is fundamentally a leadership job where nobody reports to you, and the only thing that matters is whether the product works
- Hire experienced executives deliberately and slowly, paced to your ability to integrate them; both "never hire seniors" and "hire all seniors fast" are wrong

## Notable Perspectives

> "The psychological muscle you have to build to be a great leader is to be able to look in the abyss and go, 'Okay, that way's slightly better. We're going to go that way.'"

> "If everybody agrees with the decision, then you didn't add any value because they would've done that without you. So the only value you ever add is when you make a decision that most people don't like."
