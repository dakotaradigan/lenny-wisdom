# Gibson Biddle

Former VP of Product at Netflix and CPO at Chegg. Now a full-time product leadership educator who has taught an estimated 500K-1M product leaders through talks, workshops, and writing. Author of the most popular Medium posts on product strategy.

## Key Frameworks

### DHM Model (Delight, Hard-to-Copy, Margin-Enhancing)
Product strategy should aim to delight customers in hard-to-copy, margin-enhancing ways. Delight means 10X better experiences (not just satisfying). Hard-to-copy advantages include brand, network effects, unique technology (personalization), economies of scale, and switching costs. Margin-enhancing means the strategy must also build the business. The hardest part is balancing delight against margin through A/B testing and retention metrics.

### GEM Framework (Growth, Engagement, Monetization)
A prioritization model where leaders must force-rank Growth, Engagement, and Monetization for their company's current stage. Growth = year-over-year customer growth. Engagement = product quality measured by a proxy metric like retention. Monetization = turning customers into revenue. Getting leaders to agree on this ranking is the number one way to resolve misalignment in startups. The ranking typically shifts over time.

### Two Percenters Rule
If a feature is only used by 2% of customers, kill it. Features with low reach create complexity that compounds over time (other teams forget about them, they create maintenance burden). Always evaluate ideas by asking what percentage of users will actually engage with them before investing.

## Core Ideas

- Netflix product strategies were treated as high-level hypotheses to be tested; roughly 6 out of 10 failed, but the 3-4 that succeeded created enormous value, so the key is generating many hypotheses and testing rigorously
- Most decisions PMs agonize over are actually low-stakes: check the magnitude relative to the business and whether the decision is reversible (two-way door vs one-way door) before escalating your anxiety
- Building a personal board of directors is the most impactful career habit; start by keeping up with former colleagues, then find mentors by being helpful to them first rather than asking to be mentored
- A simple, focused product experience that improves retention is more valuable than adding clever features with limited reach; Netflix killed advertising and used DVD features in 2008 to stay maniacally focused on personalization
