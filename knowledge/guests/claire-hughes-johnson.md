# Claire Hughes Johnson

Former COO of Stripe and longtime executive at Google. Author of Scaling People, which distills her experience building the operational foundations of one of the most successful technology companies. Known for her practical, systems-oriented approach to company building.

## Key Frameworks

### Founding Documents
Every company needs codified documents that capture mission (why you exist), long-term goals (3-5 aspirational objectives), and operating principles (distilled behaviors from the most effective people). These create alignment and stability as the company scales. They should be living documents that evolve but provide a stable foundation.

### Company Operating System
Goals (OKRs or similar), quarterly business reviews (QBRs), metrics dashboards, and planning processes form the operating system. The cadence matters: too frequent reviews create overhead that slows velocity; too infrequent reviews produce stale content. Look at real-time dashboards live in meetings rather than creating special presentations to reduce preparation burden.

### Type One / Type Two Decisions
Borrowed from Bezos: evaluate whether a decision is high-impact and irreversible (Type 1) or low-impact and reversible (Type 2). Most decisions are Type 2 and should be made quickly. If you don't know who the decision maker is, it's probably you -- act accordingly rather than slowing the company down.

## Core Ideas

- The COO role is not a silver bullet. Fewer than 20-30% of companies have one, and the best COO-CEO relationships have "just the right amount of tension" -- not all harmony, but mutual trust with productive friction.
- Communication requires a multi-channel strategy just like marketing: email, video, all-hands, newsletters, Slack. Never assume one communication reaches the audience. Leaders must be the "repeater in chief."
- Offsites work because yanking people out of routine activates new parts of the brain and imprints memory. Group experiences cement belief systems and plans more effectively than any document.
- If you are a PM unsure who the decision maker is, assume it is you. Being a force for positive momentum is the single best career advice.
