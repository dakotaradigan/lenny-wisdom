## Key Frameworks

### Coaching vs Advising Decision
Leaders default to advising (having the answer) because that is what got them promoted. But coaching -- getting curious and helping people solve their own problems -- is more scalable and more motivating. Advise when there is urgency or the person lacks the skill; coach for everything else. Over-advising trains teams to bring you every hard problem rather than solving them independently.

### Conflict as Mutual Understanding
The goal of any difficult conversation is not to convince the other person they are wrong but to create mutual understanding. People come into conflict ready to prove their point, but tech professionals have feelings despite the industry's logical veneer. Reframing conflict as understanding-seeking rather than winning transforms the dynamic.

### Strengths-Based Energy Management
When people operate from their natural gifts and strengths, they have more energy and are more resilient against burnout. It is your job, not your manager's, to navigate your career toward your strengths. Managers help you perform in the job you were hired for; living in your gifts is your own responsibility.

## Core Ideas

- The biggest leadership gap across all levels is knowing when to coach versus when to tell; even CEOs of public companies struggle with this distinction
- Vulnerability in seeking coaching help is hard but the audience most committed to growth benefits the most from it
- Professionals at every level need permission to acknowledge that work is emotional, not purely logical -- creating that space unlocks better performance and relationships
- Leaders who invest in understanding the talent around them and creating environments for success see their businesses thrive as a natural consequence
