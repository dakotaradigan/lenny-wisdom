# Geoff Charles

VP of Product at Ramp, the fastest-growing SaaS and FinTech business in history, reaching $100M+ ARR in two years with fewer than 50 people in R&D. Built competitors to Amex in three months, Expensify in six months, and Bill.com in three months with three engineers, one designer, and one PM.

## Key Frameworks

### Velocity as Organizing Principle
Velocity is not just a value -- it is how Ramp designs product development, incentivizes teams, decides who to hire and promote, and makes decisions. High velocity creates positive selection for talent (top people want to join companies that ship fast), de-risks decisions (low cost of being wrong), and compounds through iteration.

### Single-Threaded Small Teams
Give a small team (3-5 people) one goal, shield them from organizational chaos, and set a lofty timeline. No production engineering rotations, no documentation duties, no customer escalations for the core team. Layers of "protective tissue" (product operators, rotational engineers) absorb distractions so the core team stays focused.

### Context Over Control
Align on goals, hypotheses, and data interpretation -- not solutions. Most leadership mistakes come from being prescriptive about solutions without aligning upstream. When things go wrong, it is usually because the leader jumped to solutions without sharing the context that led them there. Teams closer to the ground produce better solutions when given the right context.

## Core Ideas

- Debate hypotheses and data interpretation, not solutions; junior leaders waste time arguing about solutions when the real disagreement is upstream
- Use market comparables (publicly traded competitors) and design prototypes (Figma walkthroughs) as dual motivation tools for ambitious goals
- Empower teams by publishing the "API" for how they interact with leadership: strategy alignment, risk flagging, and one-way decision escalation
- Revenue-driven goals are extremely motivating when combined with visible market opportunity from public competitors
- Shield new product bets from the rest of the organization until they find traction; do not even tell other teams until product-market fit emerges

## Notable Perspectives

> "Teams that have high velocity are able to actually get to that impact over time by iterating."

> "Whenever things went wrong at Ramp, it was when I was being prescriptive with regards to the solution without actually explaining and aligning upstream on the goal, the hypothesis, and the data."
