# Daniel Lereya

Chief Product and Technology Officer at monday.com, joined at ~40 employees and helped scale to 2,500 people, 250,000 paying customers, and $1B ARR. Led a pivotal transformation from output-focused to impact-focused product development after realizing competitors were shipping dramatically faster.

## Key Frameworks

### Ambitious Goal-Setting as Thinking Reset
When competitors shipped 30 column types while monday.com had built only 5 (each taking 4 months), the team set an "impossible" goal of 25 columns in one month. This forced entirely different thinking -- not working harder but working smarter. They built shared infrastructure, ran a hackathon where each developer built one column in a day, and shipped 30 columns in six weeks. The key insight: goals so ambitious that working the same way cannot achieve them force genuine innovation.

### Impact Over Output
Great PMs are relentless until they validate that impact has occurred. Every team should have a clear answer to: what will be different for customers, and how will we know we moved the needle? If the answer requires words like "enhance" or "augment" without specific measurable change, the team lacks focus. Sometimes the biggest impact is not building another feature but making existing value more accessible.

### Daily Numbers Culture
Each team builds a daily automated update with all the metrics they care about, delivered via Slack. Teams live in these numbers, having conversations about what changed and why. This creates a feedback loop where everyone -- not just PMs -- is oriented around customer impact.

## Core Ideas

- If you cannot point to one specific thing that transformed your product in the last three months despite doing many things, you have a focus problem.
- Radical transparency -- sharing every metric including revenue, churn, and signups with all employees -- creates deep partnership rather than demoralization. As a public company, monday.com has PMs sign 10b5 stock plans so they can still access all data.
- Use competitors as gifts: they prove what is possible. The four-minute mile effect applies to product development -- once someone shows it can be done, the psychological barrier disappears.
- Real speed is not skipping stages or reducing quality. It is about working on the right things and thinking differently about the approach. If a goal can be achieved by sleeping in the office, it is not ambitious enough.
