# Dylan Field

Co-founder and CEO of Figma, which he started while still a student and grew into one of the most beloved and valuable design tools in the world. Known for his relentless product intuition, obsession with simplicity, and scrappy approach to early-stage growth including literally driving to customer offices and finding users in shared Lyft rides.

## Key Frameworks

### Intuition as Hypothesis Generator
Product intuition is not about knowing the right answer -- it is a hypothesis generator. Strong intuition produces better hypotheses to test, but it must still be validated through user feedback and data. Developing intuition requires deep immersion with users and constant exposure to how people actually use tools.

### Design as Art Applied to Problem Solving
Design has a creative, expressive component (art) combined with utilitarian problem-solving. If you lose the art and only solve the problem, the product lacks soul. If you only express art without solving problems, it is not design. The combination of both is what makes products truly great.

### Simplicity as Ongoing Practice
Simplicity is not a feature you ship -- it is a continuous practice of resisting complexity as a product grows. Every new feature adds cognitive load, and the most important product decisions are often about what NOT to build. Keeping Figma simple as it scales requires active, intentional effort.

## Core Ideas

- In the early days of Figma, Dylan would do anything to get users including cold-emailing, demoing in person, and literally meeting designers in shared Lyft rides. The early growth was entirely about personal hustle and scrappiness.
- The future of design in the AI era centers on craft and quality as differentiators. When more software is created by AI, the unique human contribution becomes taste, quality, and intentionality in design.
- Community is the most powerful go-to-market force. Figma's community of passionate designers spread the product virally through organizations and across companies without a sales team for years.
- Product decisions should be informed by AI but not replaced by it. AI can generate the "most obvious thing in the most obvious form" but human judgment and taste determine what is worth building and how.
