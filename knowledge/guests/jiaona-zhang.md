# Jiaona Zhang (JZ)

SVP of Product at Webflow. Stanford lecturer on product management for six years. Previously senior director of PM at WeWork, longtime product leader at Airbnb, and PM at Dropbox. Known for combining execution discipline with strategic thinking across four iconic product companies.

## Key Frameworks

### Minimum Lovable Product (MLP)
The new MVP. In a world with many options, users expect quality -- it is better to do five things with high polish than fifteen things that each feel clunky. The lovable bar depends on context: if replacing a manual spreadsheet workflow, the bar is lower; if serving designers, it is higher. Scatter "pixie dust" on a few areas where you exceed expectations rather than trying to polish everything.

### Roadmap as Story
A roadmap is not a spreadsheet with RICE scores. It is a narrative with themes that explains why a body of work matters. Write it as a doc (not a deck), articulate the themes, then link out to the living system (Jira, Linear) where the team tracks execution. When inputs change, the story changes -- that is easier to reason about than re-scoring a spreadsheet.

### OKRs: Spirit Over Numbers
Push teams to articulate qualitatively what "crushing it" looks like before fixating on metrics. Red and yellow OKRs with genuine learnings are preferable to safe green OKRs. Ambitious targets paired with quarterly milestones prevent both sandbagging and runaway sunk cost investment.

## Core Ideas

- Become known for something early in your career -- complex launches, analytics depth, technically hard problems -- then leverage that reputation to earn larger scope and more responsibility
- Validate unit economics at the outset of ambitious projects; magical thinking about scale economics is the most common way big-vision initiatives fail (Airbnb Plus being a case study)
- Across Dropbox, Airbnb, WeWork, and Webflow, the universal lesson is to understand why people love you and double down on that core, rather than chasing competitors or spreading investment too thin
- Asking for help is the single best leadership practice; it is not a sign of weakness but the fastest path to better decisions and stronger trust
