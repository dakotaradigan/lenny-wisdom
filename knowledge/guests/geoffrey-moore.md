# Geoffrey Moore

Author of Crossing the Chasm, arguably the most influential book on go-to-market strategy ever written, with over one million copies sold. His frameworks on the technology adoption lifecycle have shaped how generations of startups approach market entry. Also authored Inside the Tornado and Zone to Win.

## Key Frameworks

### Technology Adoption Lifecycle (Four Inflection Points)
(1) Early Market: Visionaries say "We believe what you believe." Sell the vision, get a marquee lighthouse customer. Every deal is a snowflake requiring custom services. (2) Crossing the Chasm: Pragmatists say "We need what you have." Sell to a specific pain using a solution model, not a product demo -- be like a doctor asking about their pain, not showing a surgery video. (3) Tornado: Mainstream says "We want what they have." Go broad, standardize the product, capture maximum market share. (4) Main Street: Commoditized product becomes a service innovation opportunity (taxis to Uber).

### The Bonfire / Bowling Pin Strategy
The tendency in the chasm is to take any customer you can find. This is like running a match back and forth under a log -- it will never light. Instead, concentrate on one small segment (kindling) until it catches fire. Then expand to adjacent segments: either the same customer with a different use case (leverage customer references) or the same use case in a different customer base (leverage partner ecosystem). Each pin must be adjacent to the last.

### Fish-to-Pond Ratio
Your first target segment should be small enough that within two years at high growth rates, you could capture 30-50% market share. That concentration causes ecosystem partners to organize around you, which is the mechanism that creates company power at the early stage.

## Core Ideas

- Using the wrong go-to-market playbook for your adoption phase will slow you down even if you execute perfectly
- Pragmatist buyers purchase what they see their peers buying; without concentrated social proof in a segment, the category goes sideways
- Ecosystem partners form around market leaders and do not form around anyone else; becoming a big fish in a small pond is the only way to attract partners early
- The seven deadly sins of crossing the chasm include targeting too broad a segment, lacking a whole product, and not having a compelling reason to buy now
- In B2B, you never need to focus on the pain when selling to early adopters (visionaries) -- they are already sold on the vision

## Notable Perspectives

> "The tendency when you're in the chasm is, 'I just need more customers.' It's like taking a match and running it back and forth under a log. It's not going to light the log."

> "Before the chasm, customers say 'We believe what you believe.' After the chasm, they say 'We need what you have.'"
