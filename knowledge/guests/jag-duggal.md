# Jag Duggal

Chief Product Officer at Nubank, one of the world's largest digital banks (bigger than Coinbase, Robinhood, Affirm, SoFi, and Lemonade combined). 80-90% of growth is word of mouth. Previously director of PM at Facebook and head of strategy for Display Ads at Google.

## Key Frameworks

### Sean Ellis Score as a Gate
Nubank rarely scales a product until the Sean Ellis score hits a compelling threshold. In Brazil, they raised the bar from 40% to 50% "very disappointed" due to cultural politeness. They segment results to find the bullseye cohort that truly loves the product, then systematically expand what makes that cohort special to a wider user base.

### Fundamentally Different, Not Incrementally Better
Don't try to be a slightly better version of incumbents. Redefine the category on quality, complexity, and price -- ideally all three simultaneously. Break trade-off constraints that the industry assumes are fixed.

### Call 10 Customers Directly
Skip the formal research process. Have PMs pick up the phone and call 10 customers from the target segment. By the fifth call, you can predict what customers six through ten will say. This direct signal, including tone of voice, beats any statistical analysis for fine-grain insights.

## Core Ideas

- The first value of Nubank is "We want our customers to love us fanatically." This is not a sign on the wall -- 80-90% of employees can recite all five values. Culture must be the most alive thing in employees' minds.
- "Good enough isn't good enough. Is it great enough?" Great enough means customers will tell their friends unprompted. This standard eliminates the need for heavy marketing spend.
- Use Amazon mock press releases before writing a line of code. If you can't explain in two paragraphs why a target customer should care, there's still work to do.
- Product and design reviews happen several times a week asking: Why is this fundamentally different? How does this redefine the category? What breaks the usual trade-off constraints?
