# Stewart Butterfield

Founder of Slack (sold to Salesforce in one of the biggest tech acquisitions) and Flickr. Both companies famously pivoted from failed game projects. Rarely does podcasts. Known for extraordinary product taste, obsessive attention to craft, and the legendary "We Don't Sell Saddles Here" memo. Worked with CTO Cal Henderson for 23 years across both companies.

## Key Frameworks

### Utility Curves
An S-curve where the horizontal axis is cost/effort and the vertical axis is value. Early investment produces little value, then there is a steep zone of enormous payoff, then diminishing returns. Most features are treated as binary (have it or do not), but the real question is: are you on the first flat part (not enough investment), the steep part (maximum leverage), or the second flat part (diminishing returns)? Underinvestment is often the reason features fail, not the concept itself.

### The Umbrella Test (Taste as Competitive Advantage)
Walking in Vancouver rain, Stewart noticed only one-third of people tilt their umbrella to avoid poking passersby. Three explanations: malice (rare), helplessness ("I wish I could do something"), or obliviousness. Your failure to exercise courtesy is my opportunity -- just as "your margin is my opportunity." Slack grew through genuine word of mouth because small courtesies and delights created emotional connections that made people advocate for the product when changing companies.

### Don't Make Users Think
The goal is not reducing friction or number of clicks -- it is preventing users from having to think. If software asks you to make a decision you do not understand, it makes you feel stupid. Focus on comprehension over click reduction. Friction that aids understanding is actually good.

## Core Ideas

- In 2014, the year Slack launched, Stewart described the product as "a giant piece of shit" -- if you cannot see limitless opportunities to improve, you should not be designing the product
- "Divine discontent" (Bezos's term): the utility curve itself moves upward over time as user standards rise, requiring continuous reinvestment in features that seem "done"
- Hyperrealistic work-like activities: organizations spend enormous energy on things that look like work but produce nothing -- Stewart coined this term for the busy-but-unproductive phenomenon
- Most product features get implemented once and improved infrequently or never, despite user standards continuously rising
- Slack's growth came from people changing jobs: startup A uses Slack, person leaves for startup B that does not use Slack, person evangelizes because of genuine emotional connection

## Notable Perspectives

> "If you can't see almost limitless opportunities to improve, then you shouldn't be designing the product."

> "In the long run, the measure of our success will be the amount of value that we create for customers."

> "Friction in a product experience is actually often a good thing -- the challenge is really comprehension."
