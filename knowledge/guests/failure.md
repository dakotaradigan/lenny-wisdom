# Failure (Compilation Episode)

A special compilation episode drawing failure stories from over 100 podcast episodes. Features Katie Dill (Airbnb/Stripe), Paul Adams (Intercom), Tom Conrad (Pets.com/Quibi), Sri Batchu (Ramp), JZ (Airbnb/Webflow), and Gina Gotthilf (Duolingo/Latitud).

## Key Frameworks

### Earn Trust Before Driving Change (Katie Dill)
When joining as a new leader, do not come in swinging with changes. One month into her role at Airbnb, the design team staged an intervention reading from prepared papers about everything she was doing wrong. The core theme: she had not earned their trust. The fix was to shift from inflicting change on people to bringing them along. Within months, the team had the best engagement scores in the company.

### Fail Conclusively (Sri Batchu)
Growth experiments have roughly a 30% success rate. When an experiment fails, ensure it fails conclusively so the learning is durable. Maximize the treatment effect by throwing every plausible tactic at the hypothesis at once. If it still fails with maximum effort, the hypothesis itself is invalidated and the company can permanently move on rather than relitigating the same idea every time a new executive arrives.

### The A-Side / B-Side Career (Gina Gotthilf)
Every career has an impressive highlight reel (A-side) and a hidden messy reality (B-side). Between the highlights of working at Duolingo and meeting President Obama, Gina was fired, laid off, lost her visa multiple times, dropped out of college from depression, and went unpaid for six months. Recognizing B-moments as temporary rather than terminal is essential for career resilience.

## Core Ideas

- Products built from a place of competitive fear rather than genuine user empathy tend to fail, as Google's social products (Buzz, Google+) demonstrated
- The fundamental equation of a business matters more than product execution; if the underlying math is broken, no amount of iteration can save it (Quibi lesson)
- Jumping to solutions before understanding the real user problem is the most common PM mistake; Airbnb Plus failed because the team was solution-first and competitor-afraid
- Unit economics must be validated at the beginning, not assumed to work at scale through magical thinking
- Failure experiences often become the dominoes that open doors to bigger career opportunities
