# Noah Weiss

Chief Product Officer at Slack, where he has spent seven years. Previously Head of Product at Foursquare and PM at Google and Fog Creek Software. Deep experience working with product-minded founders (Stewart Butterfield, Dennis Crowley) and integrating AI into established products.

## Key Frameworks

### U-Curve of Founder Involvement
The founder/CEO should be heavily involved at the start (strategic buy-in, goals, anti-goals) and the end (quality taste-testing) of projects, with a valley of autonomy in the middle where the team explores and builds. Skipping the final quality check causes more pain post-launch than investing in it upfront.

### Complaint Storms
Structured sessions where the team collectively identifies and catalogs product frustrations -- a targeted alternative to broad brainstorming that surfaces the specific friction points worth addressing. Used at Slack to maintain product quality and identify improvement opportunities.

### Principle Alignment Before Execution
Distill product taste and intuition into shared principles that become the common language of the company. Without explicit principles, every review feels Goldilocks -- teams cannot calibrate quality expectations and the founder becomes a bottleneck saying "not quite right" without a constructive vocabulary.

## Core Ideas

- The biggest lesson from Foursquare was that "a person sharing a moment" beat "a person talking about a place" as the atomic unit of social -- this framing mistake let Instagram win the social layer
- When Slack's self-serve business plateaued in 2019, the turnaround came from incubating new teams to explore adjacent opportunities (huddles, clips) that customers were demanding
- The best approach with product-minded founders is collaborative bug bashes where the whole development team and founder sit together making the product better, rather than a handoff where the founder judges
- Big boulder bets -- incubating new teams from scratch before an area matures -- are how established products avoid getting stuck optimizing on one hill while missing a mountain range behind it

## Notable Perspectives

> "Take bigger boulder bets. Teams can often get lost crawling up that hill, not realizing that there's a huge, incredibly beautiful range behind it."

> "Using the software is so different than looking at what the software will visually appear."
