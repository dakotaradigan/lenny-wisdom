# Nir Eyal

Author of *Hooked: How to Build Habit-Forming Products* and *Indistractable: How to Control Your Attention and Choose Your Life*. A leading expert on behavioral design, habit formation, and the psychology of distraction. Former Stanford lecturer and serial entrepreneur.

## Key Frameworks

### The Hooked Model
A four-step cycle for building habit-forming products: Trigger (internal or external cue) -> Action (simplest behavior in anticipation of reward) -> Variable Reward (satisfaction that keeps users engaged) -> Investment (user puts something into the product that improves it for next use). The investment phase creates switching costs and loads the next trigger.

### The Indistractable Model
Four strategies to overcome distraction, applied in order: (1) Master internal triggers -- 90% of distraction comes from internal discomfort, not external pings. (2) Make time for traction -- turn values into a timeboxed calendar; you cannot call something a distraction unless you know what it distracted you from. (3) Hack back external triggers -- remove unauthorized interruptions. (4) Prevent distraction with pacts -- pre-commitments as a last line of defense.

### The 10-Minute Rule
When tempted by distraction, set a timer for 10 minutes. During that time, either return to the task or "surf the urge" -- observe the emotional wave crest and subside. Nine times out of ten, the urge passes. Over time, extend to 12, 20 minutes. This proves personal agency without requiring abstinence.

## Core Ideas

- Distraction is not the opposite of focus -- it is the opposite of traction. Both are actions. The difference is intent. Anything done on your schedule with intent is traction; everything else is distraction, including "productive" tasks like checking work email.
- Procrastination is an emotion regulation problem, not a character flaw. The root cause of 90% of distraction is internal discomfort -- boredom, anxiety, fatigue, uncertainty. High performers feel the same discomfort but use it as rocket fuel toward traction.
- To-do lists are counterproductive because they have no constraints and no feedback loop. They cause the planning fallacy (tasks take 3x longer than estimated) and reinforce a negative self-image. Timeboxed calendars with values-based scheduling are far more effective.
- Technology is not hijacking your brain. About 95% of people are not pathologically addicted -- they are distracted. The real issue is a lack of tools for managing internal triggers, not evil technology. Blaming technology is disempowering.
- Schedule syncing with your manager ("here's my calendar, here are tasks I can't fit -- help me prioritize") is far more effective than learning to say no. It creates transparency, builds trust, and ensures the right work gets done.
