# Karri Saarinen

Co-founder and CEO of Linear, the fastest-growing issue tracking tool beloved by modern software teams. Previously founding designer at Coinbase and principal designer at Airbnb. Built Linear with only one product manager, no AB tests, no metrics-based goals -- instead relying on taste, opinions, and craft. Linear has been profitable for two years with net negative lifetime burn.

## Key Frameworks

### Craft Through Ownership
Give project teams (engineering + design) full ownership rather than routing decisions through PMs. When engineers spend time thinking about the product -- not just executing -- they notice micro-interactions (like menu safe zones) that distinguish good-enough from delightful. Leadership must value and create space for this or it will not happen.

### Ship Early, Polish Before GA
Get features into the app within the first week, visible only internally. Then find 1-10 customers willing to co-create with early, janky versions. Polish and craft investment happens before general availability, not before any feedback. Craft is not perfection upfront -- it is rapid iteration with a quality gate before broad release.

### Opinionated Software
Build software that has a point of view on how work should be done rather than trying to accommodate every workflow. Opinions create friction for some but create delight and efficiency for many. The Linear Method emphasizes consistent cycles, clear project ownership, and communication architecture that does not require a PM layer.

## Core Ideas

- With one head of product and no other PMs, engineers and designers take on scoping, communication, and prioritization -- hire for product thinking, not just technical skill
- Paid work trials (candidates work alongside the team for several days) reveal cultural and craft fit far better than traditional interviews
- Quality is growth: every paper cut in a collaboration tool compounds friction across entire organizations, so craft directly drives retention and expansion
- The design quality bar keeps rising across all software categories; what was acceptable five years ago looks legacy today

## Notable Perspectives
