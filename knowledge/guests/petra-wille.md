# Petra Wille

Independent product leadership coach and author of Strong Product People. For over a decade has helped product teams boost their skills, with one-on-one coaching of 130+ leaders and influence reaching an estimated 50,000+ product people. Focuses specifically on the under-served layer of first-line managers and directors who lead product people.

## Key Frameworks

### Eight-Legged PM Competency Model
Product managers need far more than a T-shaped profile. The role demands competence across at least eight dimensions: understanding business problems, user problems, solution design, shipping, product discovery, data analysis, iteration, and stakeholder communication. Non-PM-background leaders who manage PMs often underestimate this complexity.

### Coaching the Coaches
The book Strong Product People is a meta-level resource: not how to do product management, but how to help product managers get better. Structured around five parts and 28 chapters covering how to coach PMs on discovery, team motivation, feedback, and professional growth.

### Storytelling as Career Accelerator
Getting promoted is significantly harder without decent storytelling and public speaking ability. Some teams compensate by having the whole team help create narratives, but individual PMs who cannot tell compelling stories face a career ceiling. This is a coachable skill, not an innate talent.

## Core Ideas

- The most common gap she sees: product people at the IC level have to learn everything on their own because their non-PM-background managers cannot curate their development or point them to the right resources
- Product management is not a role, it is a career -- there is so much to learn and get good at that it takes years of practice, not just reading books and taking courses
- Leaders who came from engineering, marketing, or business backgrounds and now manage PMs often lack empathy for the specific struggles of the PM role because they have never done it
- The most impactful coaching happens during specific transitions and moments of dilemma, not through general ongoing mentorship
