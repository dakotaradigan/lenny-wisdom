## Key Frameworks

### Trust Deficit in Indian Markets
India has a fundamentally different relationship with trust than Western markets. Low per-capita income combined with high risk aversion means consumers require much more proof before adopting new products or services. Building trust is the single most important go-to-market challenge, and brands that solve it unlock enormous scale.

### DAU vs. ARPU Challenge
Indian startups can quickly grow daily active users but struggle with average revenue per user. The population is large and digitally connected but willingness to pay is constrained by income levels and the expectation that digital services should be free or heavily subsidized. This creates a structural tension between scale and monetization.

### Second-Order Thinking
Most people optimize for first-order consequences (immediate results). The best founders and product thinkers systematically consider second and third-order effects. This applies to pricing, feature decisions, market entry, and competitive strategy. Philosophy background trains this kind of thinking.

## Core Ideas

- India is changing: founders are being respected, unicorns are celebrated, there is a National Startup Day, and the first generation of large profitable Indian tech companies is emerging
- Many successful Indian-born CEOs (Microsoft, Adobe, Alphabet, IBM, Starbucks) thrive because they follow the dharma of founders well -- sustaining and expanding existing principles rather than imposing entirely new ones
- The concept of time is different in India: no one has ever been paid hourly, which affects how people value convenience and time-saving products differently from Western markets
- The biggest opportunity for Indian startups is building for India's unique needs rather than copying Western models -- the problems and solutions look fundamentally different
