# Nan Yu

Head of Product at Linear, one of the most beloved and fastest-growing B2B SaaS products. Previously at Mode. Champions the philosophy that speed and quality are not actually in tension -- the best practitioners go fast because they are competent, not because they are sloppy.

## Key Frameworks

### 10% Rule for Speed
By the time 10% of your allotted time has passed, you should have a workable solution testing a key hypothesis. If you have a month, have something functional in week one. The first version is not expected to be perfect -- its job is to validate or invalidate major assumptions before you have invested too much.

### IC-First Feature Gatekeeping
Categorically refuse features that serve middle-manager reporting needs at the cost of IC workflows. This is Linear's core promise and the reason ICs love the product. Customization requested by managers to track metrics that ICs must manually fill in creates bad data and disengagement.

### Kneeling Process
Release features and then listen for feedback about mismatches with reality. When feedback reveals you thought about something wrong (not a volume signal but a correctness signal), fix it immediately. For large feature requests, look for concentrated pools of specific use cases rather than building one bloated solution.

## Core Ideas

- The false tradeoff between speed and quality comes from conflating speed with sloppiness; at the pinnacle of any craft, practitioners go faster and produce better output simultaneously
- Tie every feature decision to a real, named person with a specific workflow rather than hypothetical personas -- "Here's the first name, last name, here's their email, you can ask them"
- Linear's growth in enterprise is leading other segments because they reached a brand tipping point where large companies wanting to act like startups see Linear as being "for us"
- Most companies regret not saying no to reporting-driven feature requests sooner; when ICs disengage from a tool, the reporting data becomes garbage anyway
