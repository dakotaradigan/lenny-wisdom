# Roger Martin

World's number one management thinker (Thinkers50, 2017), professor emeritus at Rotman School of Management, and author of Playing to Win. Former dean of Rotman and co-leader of Monitor Company. Has spent 42 years in strategy consulting and is considered one of the most practical strategy minds in business.

## Key Frameworks

### Strategy Choice Cascade
Five integrated questions that constitute a strategy: (1) What is our winning aspiration? (2) Where will we play? (3) How will we win? (4) What capabilities must we have? (5) What management systems are required? These must reinforce each other -- strategy is the integrated set of choices that compels desired customer action. Everyone in the organization, not just the CEO, makes strategic choices.

### Playing to Win vs. Playing to Play
You must be either differentiated or lowest cost. If you are neither, competitors can jerk you around indefinitely -- there is no way to protect yourself. Signs you are playing to play: customers see your product as a coin flip versus competitors, or a low-cost competitor can price you out. The test is whether customers would pay a premium for your product (differentiation) or whether you can sustainably undercut everyone (low cost).

## Core Ideas

- Strategy is intellectually challenging because it is integrative (many questions must fit together) and emotionally intimidating because it requires saying no to things
- The resource-based view of the firm, which dominates business school teaching, is largely useless in practice -- it begs the question of which resources to invest in building without answering it
- 10% of S&P 500 CEOs are ex-Procter & Gamble because P&G expects strategy from brand managers four levels below the CEO, not just from the top
- The test for competitive advantage: can your competitor replicate your capabilities? Sometimes they can but won't (Walmart could have built Amazon's website but chose not to for a decade)
