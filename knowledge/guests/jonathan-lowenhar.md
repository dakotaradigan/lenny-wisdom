# Jonathan Lowenhar

Co-founder of Enjoy The Work, a firm helping founders become great CEOs through mentoring and frameworks rooted in studying how the best startups operate. Has run public company divisions, served as PE CEO, and done back-to-back startups. Ten years into studying the gap between being a great founder (grit, tenacity) and being a great CEO (hiring, financials, planning).

## Key Frameworks

### Founder vs. CEO
Being a founder is a state of being. Being a CEO is a craft with learnable skills. Founder Mode as an excuse to skip learning the CEO craft is dangerous. The best startup CEOs know when to drop into granular founder-mode detail and when to rise back into the cockpit to run the company.

### CEO Failure Mode Archetypes
Robot (suppresses emotion), Pleaser (prioritizes being liked), Perfectionist (beautiful product minutes before bankruptcy), Angry (unconscious rage projected onto team), Laissez-faire (hires and ignores), Ready-Fire-Aim (improvisational to a fault), Micromanager (trusts nobody), Riding the Brake / Riding the Gas. Ready-fire-aim is the most common.

### Magic Box Paradigm
Best acquisition outcomes come from seducing a buyer, not putting up a for-sale sign. Three stages: learn the fantasy, prove the fantasy, quantify the fantasy. Inflict pain on potential acquirers in your competitive advantage area while staying friendly and open. You are never for sale -- the buyer falls in love.

## Core Ideas

- At any given time, a startup works backwards from one of four things: exit, next fundraise, profitability, or winding down
- More companies die from suicide (co-founder dysfunction) than homicide (competitors)
- Every well-run company has an unmistakable rhythm of how it meets, communicates, and resolves bottlenecks
- Basic business planning is not bureaucracy but essential discipline; the ready-fire-aim CEO must learn to plan to survive scaling
