# Hilary Gridley

Head of Core Product at Whoop. Previously senior director of product at Big Health (digital therapeutics) and senior product marketing manager at Dropbox. Author of one of Lenny's most popular newsletter posts on becoming a super manager with AI.

## Key Frameworks

### Taking a Punch (Counter-Programming)
When someone on your team feels their ego is injured or fears a negative perception, don't litigate the past -- focus on the next action. Ask: "What is one small thing you can do that demonstrates the opposite of what you're afraid this person thinks of you?" Rooted in cognitive behavioral therapy's behavioral activation: act first, then feel better.

### Mental Model Transparency
Instead of teaching your team what leaders think, teach them how leaders think. Share weekly summaries of key conversations with senior leaders, including verbatim quotes, your interpretation, and what you'll do differently. This eliminates approval bottlenecks over time.

### Magic Questions
Replace open-ended questions with assertions ending in "Is that right?" or "Do you agree?" This forces hypothesis formation, lets others confirm or correct, builds judgment calibration, and prevents dependency on managers for answers.

## Core Ideas

- Product leadership is not about being the protagonist. Your job is to understand the CEO's vision and operationalize it -- not impose your own. Fulfillment comes from the millions of micro-decisions where your unique perspective shapes the product.
- Too many PMs play on easy mode. The hardest problems -- regulated industries, underserved populations -- need the best product minds, yet prestige pulls talent toward building for people like themselves.
- Habit formation beats education for driving team behavior change. Focus on consistency, reducing friction, and designing reward loops that are powerful, immediate, and emotional.
- When your leader makes a decision you disagree with, ask: "What would need to be true for them to be right?" Present their reasoning honestly, acknowledge disagreement, and commit to giving the idea its best shot.
