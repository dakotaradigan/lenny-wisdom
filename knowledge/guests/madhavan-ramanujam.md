Senior Partner at Simon-Kucher & Partners, the world's largest pricing strategy consulting firm. Author of Monetizing Innovation, the most widely read book on pricing strategy. Has worked with 400+ companies and 50+ unicorns on pricing, monetization, and profitable growth.

## Key Frameworks

### Willingness to Pay Conversations
Have pricing conversations with customers before building the product, not after. Use techniques like acceptable/expensive/prohibitively expensive price questions to find psychological thresholds, purchase probability scales (1-5), most/least important feature exercises, and trade-off scenarios. The core principle: you cannot prioritize a product roadmap without willingness to pay data. 20% of features drive 80% of willingness to pay.

### Leaders, Fillers, and Killers
A framework for packaging and bundling. Leaders are must-have features that drive purchase decisions (the Big Mac). Fillers are nice-to-haves that add perceived value at marginal cost (fries and Coke). Killers are features that, if bundled, would destroy the package for most customers but can be sold as add-ons to the 10-20% who want them (coffee with a burger). Use most/least exercises to identify which features fall into each category.

### Three Pricing Strategies
Every pricing strategy is one of three types: Skimming (launch premium, lower over time -- Apple), Penetration (thin margins, volume game -- Amazon), or Maximization (optimize value extraction in the medium term -- Microsoft). Pick one deliberately and build your business model around it.

## Core Ideas

- It is not just product-market fit but product-market-pricing fit; asking "do you like this product?" without "would you pay for it at this price?" produces misleadingly positive feedback
- Segmentation must be needs-based, not persona-based; the test is whether you can "act differently" toward each segment with distinct products, not just different positioning of the same product
- How you charge (pricing model) matters more than how much you charge; Michelin selling tires per mile instead of per tire transformed their business without changing the product
- Behavioral pricing tactics like decoy products, compromise effects, and pennies-a-day framing can drive 30%+ MRR increases with zero product changes
- 72% of innovations fail commercially because companies treat pricing as an afterthought instead of designing products around the price
