# Jessica Livingston

Co-founder of Y Combinator, the first and most famous startup accelerator (5,000+ companies funded, 200+ unicorns including Airbnb, Stripe, DoorDash). Author of Founders at Work. Known as the "Social Radar" -- her nickname at YC for her uncanny ability to read people during 10-minute interviews.

## Key Frameworks

### The Social Radar
While YC's three deeply technical co-founders evaluated technology and ideas, Jessica watched for social cues: Do the co-founders get along? Is one interrupting the other? Are they truly committed or hedging? Will they actually quit their day jobs? Are they getting defensive? These signals were invisible to her co-founders but often predicted startup success or failure.

### Signs of Hustle
The Airbnb story: Paul Graham hated their idea and tried to get them to change it. But Joe brought out the Obama O's and Cap'n McCain's cereal boxes they'd created to fund the company. That signaled: these founders will do whatever it takes. Desperation and resourcefulness trump a perfect idea.

### Balancing Technical and Social Evaluation
The best investment decisions combine deep technical assessment with social/psychological evaluation. Paul would get so excited about ideas that he'd start brainstorming improvements and want to fund immediately. Jessica would pump the brakes: "These two founders work at Google and haven't said they're quitting. That's not good."

## Core Ideas

- Jessica's contribution to YC has been systematically underrecognized. Paul Graham wrote: "Much of what's novel about YC is due to Jessica Livingston. If you don't know her, you don't understand YC." Some of this erasure stems from people who want to maintain a narrative that YC was founded only by men.
- The biggest red flags in founder evaluation: defensiveness when challenged, co-founders who don't let each other speak, hedging on commitment (not willing to quit their job), and lack of deep knowledge about their own product.
- Founders need a certain desperation -- you have to burn the boats. The best early YC companies had founders who had no fallback plan and would do anything to make it work.
- People within YC and the alumni network know what Jessica did. External recognition matters less than the impact on the people directly involved.
