# Matt Abrahams

Professor at Stanford GSB teaching communication and public speaking. Host of the Think Fast, Talk Smart podcast. Author of Think Faster, Talk Smarter. Coaches executives and leaders one-on-one on communication skills.

## Key Frameworks

### Dare to Be Dull
From improv: strive for connection over perfection. When you stop trying to give the best answer and just answer the question, you free up cognitive bandwidth that was consumed by self-evaluation. Paradoxically, this produces better communication because you are more present and connected. The opposite of play is not work -- it is fear.

### Visualization for Desensitization
Picture yourself in the speaking environment ahead of time: walking to the stage, delivering the talk, seeing it well received, stepping off. This dress rehearsal -- even done virtually in your mind -- makes the event not new and novel, giving you a sense of agency. Athletes use this routinely and research from the 1980s confirms its effectiveness.

### Reframe Anxiety as Excitement
The body has essentially one arousal response; the difference is how you label it. Saying "I'm excited to share my perspective" instead of "I'm nervous" causes you to relax by definition. Research shows people who reframe are perceived as communicating better because the pressure is lifted.

## Core Ideas

- Prepare to be spontaneous: practice structures for Q&A, toasts, feedback, and small talk so you have frameworks ready when called upon
- Use a personal mantra before speaking: "I have value to add" or "Last time this went well" to counter catastrophizing
- Speaking anxiety is the normal human condition, not a personal deficiency; even the most confident-looking leaders experience it
- Name your inner critic (e.g., "Jim") and talk back to it when it offers unhelpful narratives
- Structure spontaneous responses with frameworks like What-So What-Now What or Problem-Solution-Benefit
