Runs Modern Sales Pros, a 30,000-member sales operations community. Background in product marketing and PM, not traditional sales.

## Key Frameworks

### Founder-Led Sales Sequence
The book serves as a sequel to Lean Startup: after validating the problem and building an MVP, you must get someone to pay for it reliably and scalably. Founders cannot outsource this for the first couple dozen customers because (1) the product-market feedback loop must stay in one brain, (2) no one else has your domain expertise, and (3) you need to package the selling process before handing it to a hire.

### Innovation vs Distribution Race
Startups versus incumbents is a race: can innovation get to distribution before distribution gets to innovation? It is far easier for a founder to get minimally viable at selling than for a hired salesperson to match your domain expertise. This is why the first VP of Sales often gets fired -- the founder skipped the step.

### Modern Sales as Measured Behavior
Sales is no longer Glengarry Glen Ross. Modern sales is measurable, manageable, and improvable -- like going from licking your finger in the wind to having Mixpanel for customer behavior. CRMs and digital activity (email, calendar, Zoom) create data that enables the same rigor product teams apply to user analytics.

## Core Ideas

- The #1 misconception is that sales requires some innate gift; it is a set of learnable behaviors that any thoughtful founder can develop to a minimum viable level
- Sellers are microeconomic agents who bring supply to demand rather than waiting for demand to find supply -- they find people who should want your product and elucidate why
- Founder-led selling improves your product because you maintain the direct feedback loop with customers rather than playing telephone through a salesperson
- B2B organizations scale primarily by adding more salespeople who have customer-facing meetings, not through viral marketing loops like consumer products
