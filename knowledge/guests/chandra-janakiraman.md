# Chandra Janakiraman

Chief Product Officer and EVP at VRChat. Previously product leader at Meta, first CPO at Headspace (where he transformed it from a meditation app to a health and wellness platform), GM at Zynga, and Senior PM at Amazon. Battle-tested his strategy playbook five to six times across these companies.

## Key Frameworks

### The Five-Phase Strategy Playbook (Small-s Strategy)
An 8-12 week repeatable process for developing product strategy: (1) Preparation (4 weeks): form a strategy working group, gather behavioral insights, UXR synthesis, leadership interviews, competitive analysis, adjacent roadmaps, and user observations. (2) Strategy Sprint (1 week): share out all inputs, generate and cluster problems, flip to opportunities, rank on four criteria, select three strategic pillars. (3) Design Sprint (1 week): generate illustrative concepts that bring the strategy to life. (4) Document Writing (1-2 weeks): weave everything into a cohesive 3-4 page strategy doc. (5) Rollout (2-3 weeks): pre-flight with gatekeepers, then roadshows of 8-10 people.

### The Four Ranking Criteria for Strategic Pillars
When selecting which opportunity areas to invest in: (1) Expected impact on what matters to the business, (2) Certainty of impact based on evidence quality, (3) Clarity of levers to actually solve the problem, and (4) Differentiation of those levers for your specific team or company. Score collaboratively as a working group, even qualitatively, because the debate itself builds alignment.

### The Fruit Story (Leadership Interviews)
Imagine presenting a mango to a reviewer who hates mangos, then an apple they stopped eating, then a banana they have always disliked. This cycle of rejection happens because the strategy creator never asked the leader what they wanted. Leadership interviews before strategy creation dramatically reduce misalignment and are welcomed by leaders who enjoy the creative engagement.

### Small-s vs. Big-S Strategy
Small-s strategy is present-forward and problem-focused, operating on a two-year horizon. Big-S strategy adds an aspirational, future-back component asking what the product looks like in five to ten years and why the world is better. Both share the same process but differ in time horizon and the role of aspiration.

## Core Ideas

- Anyone can build product strategy through a clear, procedural approach. The perception that strategy requires an innate "strategy gene" is false and harmful. Breaking down the mystique makes this capability widely accessible.
- The strategy working group (engineering, product, design, data at minimum) collectively creates the strategy. This is not a solo PM exercise. The collaborative generation of problems, clustering, and ranking builds deep organizational buy-in because people naturally accept what comes from them.
- The newspaper headline technique forces the team to articulate their winning aspiration in simple, layperson language focused on real-world impact rather than technical jargon. Everyone generates headlines independently, then common themes are blended into a shared aspiration.
- A strategy document should not include a roadmap. Strategy is a companion to the roadmap, sitting between mission/vision and the plan. Including a roadmap conflates the two and undermines the strategy's shelf life of approximately two years.
- Strategy is like resonance in physics: when you apply the right frequency to an object near its natural frequency, you see disproportionate amplification. Strategy is selecting that frequency to achieve resonance between your product and the market.
