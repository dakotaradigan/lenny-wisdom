# Edwin Chen

Founder and CEO of Surge AI, the fastest company in history to reach $1B in revenue -- in under four years with fewer than 100 employees, completely bootstrapped and profitable from day one. Surge teaches AI models what is good and bad, powering training at every frontier AI lab. Previously worked at big tech companies where he concluded 90% of people could be fired and teams would move faster.

## Key Frameworks

### Quality as Deep Measurement
Most people think you can throw bodies at data labeling and get good results. That is completely wrong. Quality means building thousands of signals on every worker and every task -- keyboard strokes, response speed, reviews, code standards, model-trained evaluations. Like Google Search uses signals to rank webpages, Surge uses signals to find the best-of-the-best contributors, not just filter out the worst-of-the-worst.

### Anti-Silicon Valley Company Building
Never raise VC. Never play the PR and fundraising hamster wheel. Let customers find you through 10x better product and word-of-mouth from researchers who understand quality. Early customers aligned with your mission give better feedback than customers attracted by hype.

## Core Ideas

- AI labs' values will shape model behavior. The most important question is not "how smart is the model" but "what are we optimizing the model for." Optimizing for engagement produces AI slop; optimizing for user productivity produces models that say "your email is fine, just send it."
- We are teaching models to chase dopamine instead of truth by optimizing for the types of people who buy tabloids at grocery stores. Instead of building AI that cures cancer and solves poverty, we risk building the most sophisticated entertainment machine ever.
- Companies with even crazier revenue-per-employee ratios (like $100B per employee) will emerge in the next few years. The era of giant organizations is ending -- fewer employees means less capital, which means founders who are great at technology and product rather than pitching and hyping.
- The specific quality of training data is what differentiates frontier models. A poem that "checks all the boxes" is fundamentally different from a poem that makes you emotional -- and building systems to distinguish between them is the core technical challenge.
