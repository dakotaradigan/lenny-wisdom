# Jason Fried

Co-founder and CEO of 37signals, makers of Basecamp and HEY. Profitable for 24 consecutive years with ~75 employees, 100K+ paying customers, and double-digit millions in annual profit. No investors, no board, no plans to go public or sell. A vocal advocate for an alternative path to the venture-backed startup model.

## Key Frameworks

### The Stay Up (Not Startup)
Starting a business is easy; staying in business is the hard part. Celebrate endurance, not just launches. Plateaus are normal. The question isn't "Can I start?" but "Do I want to keep doing this when growth stalls?"

### Two-Person Feature Teams
Every feature at 37signals is built by exactly two people: one programmer, one designer, with a maximum of six weeks. No 18-person meetings, no complex decision chains. Constraints produce better work and keep the company honest.

### Underdo the Competition
Don't try to outdo competitors with more features -- it's expensive and defensive. Instead, underdo them with simplicity and clarity. One price, one product, one code base. Nobody can pay Basecamp more than $299 regardless of company size, which eliminates enterprise complexity.

## Core Ideas

- Silicon Valley has found a way to make the most profitable business model (software) the least profitable. Software margins should be 80-90%, but most VC-backed companies barely break even because they hire too many people and spend too much on acquisition.
- Success means "Would I want to do this again?" Not revenue targets, not growth goals, not OKRs. If the work was enjoyable and collectively profitable, it was successful.
- Financial goals, OKRs, and KPIs are absent at 37signals. The only metric that matters: did we make more money than we spent this year?
- Hiring 500 people wouldn't make 37signals better -- it would make it worse. The company would fall apart under the organizational complexity. More people means more layers, more communication overhead, and more decisions that don't need to be made.
- Energy from work is a crucial signal. When the business gives you energy, keep going. When it saps energy, it may be time to change something. This fluctuates naturally over a 25-year career.
