## Key Frameworks

### Behavioral Diagnosis
Map out every single step required for a desired behavior. When a FinTech app tested budgeting (their most requested feature), it had zero impact on spend -- because the behavioral steps (know budget, track position in month, plan, execute repeatedly) are overwhelmingly difficult. The gap between what people say they want and what they actually do is where behavioral science adds value.

### Default > Effort
The first line of defense for any behavior change is making the desired behavior the default. Automatic 401(k) enrollment is why America has retirement savings. When defaults are not possible, create rules of thumb (heuristics) that replace in-the-moment deliberation: "I don't take Lyft on weekdays" is easier to follow than "is this $15 ride worth it right now?"

### Predictable Irrationality
People make decisions with emotion, present bias, and social norm following -- but in predictable ways. Once you understand these patterns, you can design for them. The key biases: present bias (overweighting immediate experience), social norms (following what others do), loss aversion, and status quo bias.

## Core Ideas

- The most requested feature is often not the most impactful; behavioral diagnosis reveals why -- the feature may address a stated preference but not the actual behavior bottleneck
- Any work put on the user should be viewed skeptically; you must prove it is worth their cognitive effort and then measure whether they actually do it
- Behavior change is driven by what you do, not what you believe or intend; redesign the environment rather than relying on goals and motivation
- The self-help industry's biggest flaw is implying that having a goal is enough; you need environmental redesign and defaults to actually change behavior
