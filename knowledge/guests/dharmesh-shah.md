# Dharmesh Shah

Co-founder and CTO of HubSpot ($30B+ company), who has zero direct reports and has never had direct reports across 7,000+ employees. Born in a village in India with no paved streets. Built Wordplay (16M users, $90K/month revenue) as a side project, bought chat.com for $15M+ and sold it for more. Known for his engineering approach to everything including humor and culture.

## Key Frameworks

### Laughs Per Minute (LPM) Engineering
Applied engineering rigor to public speaking by measuring laughs per minute using custom-built software. Transcribes practice talks, identifies laugh points, maps visual gaps where no laughter occurs, then either shortens those segments or injects humor. The punchline must literally be the last words spoken, then stop -- the audience needs a half-second to react and permission to laugh.

### Talent vs. Skill Decomposition
Talent controls the slope of the learning curve, but most things are acquirable skills. Functionally decompose any skill (like public speaking) into sub-skills, pick one sub-skill per year to improve, measure progress quantitatively. If Dharmesh can learn public speaking as a self-described introvert, anyone can learn anything.

### Culture as Code
When his co-founder asked him to "go do the culture thing," Dharmesh (who describes himself as someone who likes people but does not like being around them) approached culture like a coding problem. The result was HubSpot's Culture Code, which became one of the most shared company documents in tech.

## Core Ideas

- One of HubSpot's earliest strategic zigs was doing the opposite of standard startup advice to focus on one thing. They deliberately built a broad platform when conventional wisdom said to specialize -- and it worked because small businesses needed an integrated solution.
- Having zero direct reports as CTO of a 7,000-person company is intentional. Rather than becoming "passively okay at management," Dharmesh focuses exclusively on areas where he can have the most outsized impact.
- Side projects are not distractions -- they are learning laboratories. Building Wordplay taught lessons about consumer product dynamics that directly informed HubSpot product decisions.
- The best humor in business talks comes not from jokes but from stories with a punchline. Even better: self-deprecating humor that makes the audience feel connected rather than inferior.
