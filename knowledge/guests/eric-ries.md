# Eric Ries

Creator of the Lean Startup methodology and author of The Lean Startup, one of the most influential books on product building. Coined or popularized the terms MVP, pivot, vanity metrics, and continuous deployment. Former founder and CTO, currently founder and executive chairman of the Long-Term Stock Exchange (LTSE). Has worked with companies across every industry from jet engines to cancer therapies.

## Key Frameworks

### MVP as Hypothesis Test
MVP is not about building something cheap or low quality. It is the most efficient way to test your riskiest hypothesis. The minimum is context-dependent: for iOS apps it might be one exquisite feature, for LTSE it took 10 years. The key heuristic: write your feature list, cut it in half, cut it in half again, and build that. Most founders are off by one to two orders of magnitude in how much they think is necessary.

### The Pivot (Descriptive, Not Prescriptive)
A pivot is changing the strategy while maintaining fidelity to the vision. It is descriptive -- it names something founders already do -- not a recommendation. Successful pivots happen when teams admit what they have learned from experiments and adjust accordingly. The danger is building a high-quality wrong value proposition and mistaking craftsmanship for validation.

### Innovation Accounting
Establish a baseline measurement of where you are, then demonstrate that you can improve from that baseline through validated learning. This is the mechanism that connects experimentation to business progress. Without it, teams cannot distinguish between a product that failed because the value proposition was wrong versus one that had a JavaScript bug preventing signups.

## Core Ideas

- A startup is an experiment whether you treat it that way or not; the question is whether you learn from it systematically or accidentally
- The biggest waste in product development is building something customers do not want; no amount of code quality compensates for a wrong value proposition
- First principles thinking has two components: descriptive (naming what already happens) and prescriptive (deducing implications); the descriptive part is the real work
- People naturally overestimate what is needed for an MVP because they fear the discomfort of showing incomplete work to customers, not because the market actually demands completeness
- Founders bear responsibility for what they put into the world; "change the world" should always imply "for the better," even if it seems obvious

## Notable Perspectives
