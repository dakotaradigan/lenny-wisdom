# Claire Butler

First go-to-market hire at Figma (employee #10), where she led the original launch, branding, positioning, and bottom-up growth motion. Eight years in, she continues to lead GTM and community efforts at one of the most successful product companies in history.

## Key Frameworks

### Bottom-Up IC Champion Model
Figma's GTM motion focuses on winning over individual contributors (designers) who love the tool so much they put their own social capital on the line to spread it within their organizations. These internal champions drive enterprise adoption without sales involvement -- Figma had no sales team for three years. Revenue comes when companies organically need to organize widespread usage through procurement.

### Signal Over Metrics at Early Stage
At early stages, you cannot optimize your way to product-market fit. Small numbers make metrics unreliable. Instead, look for qualitative signals: are people pulling the laptop from your hands to try the product? Will one company use it full-time? Signal and intuition matter more than conversion rate optimization when numbers are small.

## Core Ideas

- Figma launched without multiplayer (its core differentiator) because the team needed momentum after three years in stealth, and there was enough signal that designers were excited about the core editor. Shipping before your key feature is ready can be the right call if you have enough signal.
- The first go-to-market hire at a startup has nobody to gut-check decisions with, which requires building immense confidence in your own judgment. Lean on the founder's intuition and just go for it.
- Node graphs showing how usage spreads person-to-person within organizations revealed that internal champions are the key to everything -- one person at the center of a cluster drives adoption across an entire company.
- Scrappiness is essential: the first Microsoft meeting came from sliding into a friend's ex-boyfriend's DMs after seeing they signed up for Figma.
