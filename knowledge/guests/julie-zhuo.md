# Julie Zhuo

Co-founder of Sundial (product analytics startup). Spent 13 years at Facebook as head of design for the Facebook app, joining as an IC intern and rising to VP of Design. Author of the bestselling The Making of a Manager. Writer of The Looking Glass newsletter, which has inspired a generation of product and design leaders. Over 400K social media followers.

## Key Frameworks

### Imposter Syndrome as Growth Signal
Feeling like an imposter and being in the fastest growth phase of your career are two sides of the same coin. If you are in a position where you have not done the thing before, that discomfort is the marker of intense learning. It took Julie seven to eight years at Facebook before becoming comfortable, and she now sees that period as the most transformative.

### Writing as Thinking
Started writing a weekly blog post as a New Year's resolution to overcome fear of speaking up in large meetings. The goal was simply to hit publish 52 times -- no quality bar, no audience goal. Writing became self-therapy: organizing scattered thoughts into letters to herself. Within a year, her ability to speak up in meetings improved dramatically, and the practice became clarifying for product and leadership thinking.

### NaNoWriMo Discipline for Any Writing
Set a daily word count goal (500 words, ~30-45 minutes) rather than a time goal. Separate getting words out from editing and quality. The hardest part is starting -- the blank page. This discipline scales from blog posts to 70,000-word books.

## Core Ideas

- The three tools for handling imposter syndrome: (1) remember everyone feels this way, (2) ask for help rather than faking it, (3) be vulnerable about struggles to form deeper connections and spread the problem-solving load
- As a manager, you develop your eye (ability to critique) but lose your hand (ability to produce); returning to IC work is humbling and reveals how much craft atrophies without practice
- Joining a company that is scaling creates disproportionate career opportunity because there are always more problems than people to solve them
- Data-driven product building reduces bias in intuition, helps prioritize at scale, and should be accessible to every company -- not just the Facebooks of the world
