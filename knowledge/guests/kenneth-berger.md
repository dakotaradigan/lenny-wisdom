# Kenneth Berger

Executive coach for startup leaders, helping them avoid burnout and live the life they want. First product manager at Slack. Over 20 years in tech as founder and operator before transitioning to coaching. His core teaching -- ask for what you want -- sounds simple but is the root of most career and life struggles.

## Key Frameworks

### Ask for What You Want
The core skill. Most people either do not ask at all (people-pleasing -- hoping others read their mind) or demand with entitlement (control -- ordering people around). Effective asking requires knowing what you want, stating it clearly, expecting and accepting "no," and iterating based on the response. It is an integrity practice: are you actually pursuing what matters to you?

### Complaints as Inspiration
Every complaint implies a dream. When stuck, examine your complaints and reverse-engineer the desire underneath. "My manager never gives me feedback" implies "I want regular, honest feedback." Now you have something concrete to ask for.

### Stuckness as Diagnostic
If you are in the same stuck place week after week, you are probably trying the same approach and not learning from results. The fix: treat your ask as an iterative development process. Ask, hear the response, adapt, ask differently. Product development mindset applied to personal growth.

## Core Ideas

- Being attached to being right is fundamentally disrespectful; you cannot know for certain you are right before a conversation, so entering a meeting with that assumption shuts down the dialogue
- High-stakes situations focus us on fear of what we do not want rather than pursuit of what we do want -- a critical distinction because running from fears does not move you toward goals
- Interpersonal conflict usually stems from one of two failure modes: holding back (people-pleasing) or coming in entitled (control); both are failures of asking well
- Kenneth was fired from Slack -- a formative experience that crystallized many of these lessons about integrity, asking for what you want, and the cost of not doing so
