# Graham Weaver

Teaches a top-rated course at Stanford's Graduate School of Business (2024 MBA Distinguished Teaching Award). Founder and CEO of Alpine Investors, one of the top-performing private equity funds in the world. Half of his student meetings are about the same question: "What should I do with my life?" His advice comes from someone actually doing the thing, not just teaching it.

## Key Frameworks

### The Genie Framework
Imagine finding a magic lamp. The genie says: "Whatever you throw yourself into with your whole life and career, it's going to turn out great. It'll take longer than you think, be harder than you think, but it'll work out beyond your wildest imagination." What would you wish for? That answer -- the thing you would do absent the fear of failure -- is what you should be pursuing. Maybe not tomorrow, but at some point in your one life.

### Everything Worth Having Is on the Other Side of "Worse First"
The first move toward any improvement is negative. Want a better body? The gym is not fun at first. Want a new career? The transition is painful. People hit plateaus because they optimize for tomorrow (staying comfortable) rather than for their five-year trajectory. The willingness to endure the "worse first" period separates people who grow from people who stagnate.

### The Nine Lives Exercise
Imagine you had nine separate lives to live. What would you do in each one? This exercise helps identify desires that are being suppressed by practical constraints and fear. The patterns across your nine lives reveal what you actually want.

## Core Ideas

- Most students know in their gut what they want (option B) but talk themselves into the safer choice (option A); the advisor's job is to help them feel the energy difference and deconstruct limiting beliefs
- Life is suffering regardless of what you choose, so you may as well choose suffering that is meaningful and aligned with what you actually want
- Autopilot mode means being unconscious about why you are doing what you are doing; the antidote is intentional reflection on direction and priorities
- Most worthwhile things take longer than expected; patience and sustained effort over years is the norm, not the exception
- Accountability structures (coaches, peer groups, public commitments) are essential because good intentions without systems produce no change

## Notable Perspectives

> "Everything that you want is on the other side of worse first."

> "If I'm optimizing for tomorrow and I just want to have a great day tomorrow, I'm going to stay exactly where I am."
