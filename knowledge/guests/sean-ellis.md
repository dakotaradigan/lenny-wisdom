# Sean Ellis

Head of Growth at Dropbox and Eventbrite; founding team at LogMeIn (sold for $4B+). Author of *Hacking Growth*, one of the most popular growth books of all time. Coined the term "growth hacking" and created the Sean Ellis product-market fit test used by thousands of startups worldwide.

## Key Frameworks

### The Sean Ellis Test (Product-Market Fit Survey)
Ask users "How would you feel if you could no longer use this product?" with options: very disappointed, somewhat disappointed, not disappointed. If 40%+ say "very disappointed," you have a leading indicator of product-market fit. Best administered to users who have activated and used the product at least twice within the last two weeks. Minimum 30 responses for a reliable sample.

### The Value Delivery Engine
After confirming product-market fit, map every lever that grows your north star metric: onboarding, activation, engagement loops, referral mechanics, revenue model, and acquisition channels. Optimize them in that order -- activation first, acquisition last -- because efficient conversion and retention are prerequisites for scalable acquisition.

### ICE Prioritization Framework
Score experiment ideas on Impact, Confidence, and Ease to create a systematic, transparent way to compare and prioritize growth experiments across the organization. Keeps idea sourcing democratic while making prioritization decisions defensible.

## Core Ideas

- Product-market fit is not binary -- even at 7%, drilling into the "very disappointed" users reveals which value proposition to double down on, enabling rapid improvement through repositioning and onboarding changes without changing the core product.
- Retention problems are usually onboarding problems in disguise. Getting the right users to the right experience quickly matters far more than tactical retention hacks.
- Ignore "somewhat disappointed" users -- they signal a nice-to-have. Building for them risks diluting the experience for must-have users. Focus on deepening value for passionate users first.
- North star metrics should reflect units of value delivered to customers, not revenue. Revenue should be a byproduct of delivering value. Time-bound metrics (weekly vs. monthly) change team behavior significantly.
- The blend of qualitative and quantitative research produces far better growth experiments than either alone. Ask users how they found the product and why the core benefit matters to them -- these surface the context needed for effective messaging and acquisition.
