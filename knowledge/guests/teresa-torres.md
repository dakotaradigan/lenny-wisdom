## Key Frameworks

### Opportunity Solution Tree
A visual tree starting with an outcome at the root, branching into the opportunity space (unmet needs, pain points, desires), then into solutions and assumption tests. Structure the opportunity space using an experience map with distinct moments in time. Opportunities get smaller and more solvable as you move down the tree. The key discipline: 98% of people write solutions when they think they are writing opportunities.

### Continuous Discovery Habits
Discovery and delivery happen in parallel, continuously, not as sequential phases. One customer interview per week is sufficient to start. Automate recruiting by embedding opt-in prompts in your product or leveraging internal teams already talking to customers. The goal is for interviews to appear on your calendar without effort.

### The Product Trio
Product manager, designer, and software engineer decide together. When trios work from shared understanding through joint discovery, disagreements drop dramatically. When disagreement arises, it signals the need for a better option, not a tiebreaker. This requires unlearning business culture's territorial instincts.

## Core Ideas

- Interviewing is a grossly underestimated skill; collect specific stories about recent experiences rather than asking direct questions out of context, which trigger unreliable answers
- Everything in the backlog is a bet whether you do discovery or not; discovery just helps you make better bets, so do not stop delivering while you start discovering
- If your company is a feature factory, do not try to force organizational change as an individual contributor; just change how you individually work and find customers to talk to on your own
- Compare and contrast multiple solutions for the same opportunity; we intuitively do this when apartment hunting or job searching but forget to do it in product work
- Opportunities should be specific enough to solve, not vague aspirations like "make this easier to use"
