# Paul Adams

CPO at Intercom for over ten years. Previously global Head of Brand Design at Facebook and user researcher at Google (where he worked on the failed social products Google Buzz and Google+). Known for embracing failure as a learning mechanism and for leading Intercom's all-in bet on AI for customer support.

## Key Frameworks

### AI Product Strategy Assessment
Start with what your product does at its core -- why people use it, what problem it solves. Then ask: can AI do that? For some functions it will be replacement, for others augmentation, and for some it cannot yet. This produces a clear map of where AI threatens your product and where it enhances it.

### Ship Fast, Ship Early, Ship Often
Originally called "Ship to Learn" at Intercom. Bake the expectation of failure into the culture. If you ship early and fast and learn fast, you can change and improve fast. The tension with quality standards is real and constant -- navigating it is the ongoing work, not a problem to be solved once.

### Building from Fear vs Building from Empathy
Google's social products (Buzz, Plus) failed because they were motivated by competitive fear of Facebook rather than genuine desire to solve user problems. Products built from fear of being disrupted tend to fail; products built from empathetic understanding of what people struggle with tend to succeed.

## Core Ideas

- AI is a technology shift bigger than mobile, comparable to the internet itself; leaders who do not invest time exploring AI personally will be left behind
- Surviving public failure (freezing on stage at Cannes, working on failed Google products) builds resilience and teaches you what not to do as a leader
- At Intercom, the pitch to leave Facebook was: "At Facebook you can design the product; at Intercom you can design the company" -- designing the company culture itself was the most appealing opportunity
- Big bets with high risk and high reward are more exciting and productive than incremental optimization, but require a culture that genuinely tolerates failure
