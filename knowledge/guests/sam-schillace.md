# Sam Schillace

Corporate VP and Deputy CTO at Microsoft. Creator of Google Docs through his startup Writely, which became the foundation for Google Workspace (1B+ monthly active users). Possesses the very first Google Doc ever created. A deeply creative technologist who believes innovation comes from messing around at the edges.

## Key Frameworks

### What-If vs. Why-Not Questions
When encountering something disruptive, people ask either "what if this works?" (optimistic, possibility-seeking) or "why not?" (defensive, threat-driven). Why-not questions are easy to generate but offer little upside. What-if questions -- even if most are wrong -- lead to breakthroughs. Google Docs faced endless why-nots (no connectivity on airplanes, people won't trust cloud storage) that all proved irrelevant.

### The Disruption Bifurcation Signal
If a product generates strong love AND strong hate with little indifference in between, it is truly disruptive. Bell-curve reactions (mild like, mild dislike) indicate incremental improvement. Google Docs had millions of users while Google executives were still calling it a stupid idea -- the haters outnumbered fans, but the fans were passionate enough to sustain growth.

## Core Ideas

- Every new idea looks dumb at first; unfortunately, dumb ideas also look dumb -- but if people call something "a toy," that is often a signal it is truly disruptive
- Get your tools sharp and then "just fuck around" -- make cheap experiments easy so you can try many things and notice the surprising results
- Being optimistic and wrong beats being pessimistic and right; there is not much of a prize for pessimism, especially in transformational moments like AI
- Work does not have to be hard -- do the thing you feel guilty getting paid for, and do it as intensely as possible; we tend to undervalue what comes naturally to us

## Notable Perspectives
