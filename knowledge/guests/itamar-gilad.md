# Itamar Gilad

Former product manager at Google (Gmail, YouTube) and Microsoft. Author of Evidence-Guided. Creator of the GIST model and the Confidence Meter. Led the development of Gmail's tabbed inbox feature used by 1.8 billion users.

## Key Frameworks

### GIST Model (Goals, Ideas, Steps, Tasks)
A meta-framework combining lean startup, design thinking, and growth methodology. Goals define the end state with metric trees. Ideas are hypothetical ways to achieve goals, evaluated with ICE (Impact, Confidence, Ease). Steps combine building and learning through progressive validation. Tasks are the Agile execution layer. The key insight: most organizations skip the Ideas and Steps layers, jumping from vague goals straight to tasks.

### The Confidence Meter
A visual tool scoring evidence strength from 0-10. Opinions and pitch decks score near zero. Peer review and estimates score slightly higher. Market data and surveys reach the middle. Only actual tests -- fake doors, alphas, A/B experiments -- reach medium to high confidence. Prevents teams from over-investing in low-confidence ideas.

### Metric Trees
Build two interconnected metric trees: one for value delivered (North Star metric) and one for value captured (revenue/business KPI). Where they overlap reveals the most strategically important metrics. Use these to align teams, inform org structure, and estimate experiment impact.

## Core Ideas

- Google+ is the epitome of opinion-based development: a huge bet made on conviction without evidence, consuming 1,000+ engineers for years. Gmail's tabbed inbox was the opposite -- small idea nobody believed in, validated through progressive testing, now used by billions.
- Evidence is empowering for mid-level managers. When you bring data, even skeptical leaders often listen. If they don't, polish your resume.
- The right metric is not speed of delivery but time to outcomes. Evidence-guided methods are faster at producing the right outcomes, even if they feel slower in raw output.
- Most companies that think they're evidence-guided aren't. Telltale signs: unclear goals, missing user-facing metrics, excessive roadmap planning time, low experimentation, and disengaged engineering teams.
