# Sarah Tavel

Partner at Benchmark, one of the most preeminent venture capital funds. First product manager at Pinterest where she led the discovery team (home feed, search, recommendations). Focuses on investing in consumer and marketplace startups. Thinks like a product and growth leader despite being a VC.

## Key Frameworks

### Hierarchy of Engagement (Consumer Products)
A three-level framework for building enduring consumer products. Level 1: Get users completing the core action (the one action that proves they understand and value your product -- pinning for Pinterest, subscribing for YouTube). Level 2: Make the product more retentive by ensuring it gets better with use and users have more to lose by leaving (accruing benefits + mounting loss). Level 3: Make the product self-perpetuating through network effects, growth loops, and re-engagement loops where user activity creates value for other users. Products that miss Level 3 (like Evernote) hit a ceiling.

### Hierarchy of Marketplaces
A three-level framework for building enduring marketplaces. Level 1: Focus on a constrained "thimble" market to achieve minimum viable happiness. Level 2: Tip the market by reaching saturation and triggering tipping loops (growth loops + happiness loops). Level 3: Dominate by expanding the proven playbook into adjacent markets. Work backwards from the goal of dominance -- the value of a marketplace increases exponentially with market share distance from #2.

### Happy GMV
Not all GMV is created equal. Customers do not care how big your marketplace is; they care whether each transaction makes them happy. Focus on "happy GMV" -- transactions where both sides retain and come back -- rather than raw volume. This is why spreading thin across many markets to hit GMV milestones is counterproductive.

## Core Ideas

- The core action is the single most important metric for a consumer product; at Pinterest, pinning predicted next-week return with 90%+ probability, and optimizing for it focused the entire product roadmap
- Markets should be evaluated by their currents (dynamics of change creating momentum) rather than their size; underestimated markets with strong currents and no competition are the most attractive
- Anonymity in consumer products prevents accruing benefits and mounting loss at Level 2, which is why pure anonymous apps (Secret, early Clubhouse) consistently fail to build lasting engagement
- Marketplace founders should constrain focus like a laser beam, not spread ambition like the sun warming the ocean; DoorDash won by focusing on suburbs with no competition while Postmates spread across cities and categories
- Tipping loops come in two types: growth loops (where users pull in new users) and happiness loops (where the marketplace rewards good suppliers and churns bad ones, acting as kidneys for quality)
