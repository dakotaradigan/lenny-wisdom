# Jenny Wen

Head of design for Claude Cowork at Anthropic. Previously director of design at Figma (FigJam, Slides). Also designed at Dropbox, Square, and Shopify. Living at the bleeding edge of how the design profession is transforming due to AI.

## Key Frameworks

### Two Types of Design Work in the AI Era
(1) Supporting execution: engineers ship with their seven Claude instances, and designers guide quality, polish, and coherence in real-time rather than creating mocks upfront. (2) Creating vision/direction: still essential, but now it's 3-6 month prototypes pointing teams in the right direction, not beautiful 2-5 year vision decks.

### The Shifted Pie Chart
Previously: 60-70% mocking/prototyping, 20% engineer collaboration, 10% coordination. Now: 30-40% mocking, 30-40% jamming and pairing with engineers, plus a new slice for direct implementation in code. Designers are doing last-mile polish, implementing features, and spending much less time on static mocks.

### Three Designer Archetypes for Hiring
In the current moment, the most interesting designer candidates fall into archetypes that blend traditional design skills with technical implementation ability, systems thinking, and comfort with ambiguity around non-deterministic AI outputs.

## Core Ideas

- The traditional design process (research, diverge, converge, diverge, converge) is dead. It was dying before AI, but engineers shipping with multiple AI coding agents has forced the change. Designers who cling to "trust the process" are falling behind.
- Engineers are changing design, not the other way around. Design tooling lags engineering tooling. The forcing function is that engineers can build so fast that blocking them with a mock-first process destroys velocity.
- With AI products, you cannot mock all the states. Models are non-deterministic. You discover use cases by watching people use the product, not by theorizing in Figma. Shipping and iterating with real models produces better products than traditional design processes.
- AI will get better at taste, judgment, and design. Designers may be holding onto these differentiators a bit too much. But someone still needs to be accountable for what actually gets built and what actually matters.

## Notable Perspectives

> "This design process that designers have been taught, we sort of treat it as gospel. That's basically dead."

> "It's not just designers who are feeling like, 'Oh yeah, we have to keep up with engineers.' I think even engineers are like, 'How do we keep up with ourselves?'"
