## Key Frameworks

### The Highlight Method (Make Time)
Each morning, choose one thing that if someone asked "What was the highlight of your day?" you'd want to name. Block 60-90 minutes of peak energy for it. This single practice reframes productivity from "get everything done" to "do one thing well." The four-part system: Highlight (choose it), Laser (focus tactics), Energize (sleep/food/exercise), Reflect (did it work?).

### Busy Bandwagon and Infinity Pools
Two forces conspire against meaningful work. The Busy Bandwagon is the cultural expectation that everyone should be busy -- it drives reactive behavior. Infinity Pools are endlessly replenishing content sources (email, social media, news) engineered to capture attention. Naming these forces is the first step to changing defaults around them.

### Design Your Day
Use your calendar as a canvas you design, not a thing done to you. Block focus time (ideally mornings), create team-wide calendar templates with content buckets for different activity types, and protect highlight time with the same seriousness as meetings.

## Core Ideas

- Most productivity advice is backwards: it helps you get faster at doing what's already in front of you. The real opportunity is changing defaults so the most important thing gets your best attention first.
- You don't need to do all 87 tactics in the book. If you find one or two that make you a little more productive each day, that compounds into a huge difference. Experiment, reflect, and rotate tactics every few months.
- Willpower alone will never beat the busy bandwagon and infinity pools. You need environmental design -- deleting apps, blocking notifications, creating physical and digital barriers between you and distraction.
- It's okay to be a B- at productivity. Things can be a mess outside your highlight and you still feel good about the day if you had one great moment of focused work.
