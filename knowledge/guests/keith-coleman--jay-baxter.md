# Keith Coleman & Jay Baxter

Keith Coleman is VP of Product and Jay Baxter is Founding ML Engineer and Researcher for Community Notes at X (formerly Twitter). Together they built one of the most impactful and clever products for combating misinformation on the internet -- now adopted by Meta as their primary fact-checking approach. Community Notes survived four different company leaders including Jack Dorsey and Elon Musk.

## Key Frameworks

### Bridging-Based Agreement Algorithm
Community Notes does not use majority vote. It looks for agreement from people who have historically disagreed with each other. When polarized users find surprising consensus that a note is helpful, that note is likely accurate, neutrally written, and informative. This provides strong anti-manipulation properties without requiring closed-source systems or fact-checker ground truth labels.

### Open to All Humanity
Anyone can become a Community Notes contributor (with basic verification like a phone number). The team resisted calls to limit participation to journalists or curated groups. Random selection and openness make the system feel fair and trustable. Every post is eligible -- including from Elon, government figures, and advertisers.

### Lean Team, Maximum Impact
The Community Notes team is remarkably small. Working within Elon's organization reinforced that small groups accomplish more, not less, because of reduced coordination overhead. The product's design prioritizes algorithmic scalability over human editorial decisions.

## Core Ideas

- The system was built before anyone thought it could work; a room of ML engineers in 2020 would have said you need closed-source systems and fact-checker labels, but bridging-based agreement proved them wrong
- External independent studies confirmed that posts with Community Notes change people's agreement with core claims compared to posts without notes
- The product survived multiple leadership transitions by staying true to principles: openness, algorithmic neutrality, and transparency (the algorithm is open-source)
- Classic misinformation examples: AI-generated images, out-of-context photos from different countries and time periods presented as current events
