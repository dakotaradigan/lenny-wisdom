## Key Frameworks

### Day 1 vs Day 100 Features
Day 1 premium features deliver value immediately upon first engagement and should go in your starter paid plan to maximize upgrade rates. Day 100 features represent advanced functionality whose value emerges from scale and usage over time -- push these into a higher-tier plan to reduce cognitive overload for new users and create a natural upsell path.

### Van Westendorp Willingness-to-Pay
Survey users with four price sensitivity questions: too cheap (quality concern), right price, expensive but would still pay, and prohibitively expensive. Plotting these four curves reveals an acceptable price range that balances conversion with revenue capture.

### Three Monetization Mistakes
(1) Waiting too long to charge -- you cheapen your product and miss critical feedback on willingness to pay. (2) Underpricing -- a single tier leaves money on the table from power users who would happily pay more. (3) Set it and forget it -- pricing should be revisited every 6-12 months alongside meaningful product launches.

## Core Ideas

- At Evernote, the top reason people upgraded from free to paid was guilt, which was a clear signal the free tier was too generous and the company was leaving money on the table
- Matching price to value through a usage-based metric (API calls, messages, storage) creates natural expansion revenue and reduces churn by 20-25% compared to flat pricing
- A 1% improvement in monetization has 4X the bottom-line impact of a 1% improvement in acquisition
- You cannot retrofit collaboration into a single-player product; Evernote's failure to cross the multiplayer chasm capped its growth potential
