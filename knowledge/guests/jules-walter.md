## Key Frameworks

### IQ Skills Then EQ Skills
Early career: focus on hard skills -- execution, product sense, strategy, and critically, interview skills. Later career: shift to EQ -- communication across audiences, leadership across teams, self-awareness of stress patterns, managing ambiguity. EQ skills are harder to learn because what you need to work on is specific to you.

### Interview Skills as Career Unlocked
The most underrated PM skill. Most people do zero mock interviews. Practice dozens of mocks with peers or mentors; at your worst, you should still be good enough. Interview skills are especially critical for underrepresented PMs who face additional psychological barriers in unfamiliar environments.

### Mentorship Multiplier
Build a stable of mentors who put a mirror in front of you. A mentor who identifies that you go quiet under stress (and how others interpret that) gives you actionable self-awareness that no amount of reading can provide. Seek mentors through specific challenges, not generic "be my mentor" requests.

## Core Ideas

- Getting into product management often requires joining a startup first, building PM experience there, then leveraging that for larger companies
- Communication evolves through career stages: clarity and conciseness early, then storytelling and empathetic audience adaptation later
- Self-awareness patterns (e.g., withdrawing under stress) must be identified through external feedback before they can be addressed
- Feedback on interview performance is almost never given, making mock interviews with honest peers essential for improvement
