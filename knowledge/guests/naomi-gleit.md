# Naomi Gleit

Head of Product at Meta and employee number 29 at Facebook. Longest-serving executive after Mark Zuckerberg with 19+ years at the company. Pioneer on Facebook's legendary growth team and leader of cross-functional initiatives including Teen Accounts.

## Key Frameworks

### Understand, Identify, Execute
The growth team's core operating model: first instrument all the data you need (understand), then analyze it to find the highest-leverage opportunities (identify), then build products to address them (execute). In 2009, the entire growth team stopped their roadmap for months to focus solely on data instrumentation, which unlocked every subsequent growth initiative.

### Extreme Clarity and Canonical Everything
Every project should have one canonical document that links to everything else. Canonical nomenclature ensures shared vocabulary (e.g., "consistency" vs. "accuracy" mean different things). Use numbered lists instead of bullets so items can be referenced precisely. Edit decisions into the projected visual in real-time during meetings so everyone leaves with identical understanding.

### PM as Conductor
Product managers are conductors of an orchestra, not the star performers. Each function (engineering, design, legal, policy, data) is an instrument section. The PM ensures everyone plays their part correctly, plays together in harmony, and maintains the right tempo. Conductors do not speak during the performance.

### Traffic Light Decision Framework
Present three options as rows, evaluation criteria as columns, and color-code each cell red/yellow/green. This replaces flat pros-and-cons lists with a visual that immediately reveals which option performs best across the dimensions that matter. Criteria can be functional perspectives (legal, policy, product) or optimization goals (user experience, engineering feasibility, complexity).

## Core Ideas

- The biggest value of Facebook's "7 friends in 10 days" activation metric was not its statistical precision but that it gave the entire organization a single rallying point. Having any clear shared goal is more valuable than having a perfect one.
- Growth accounting (new users minus churned users plus resurrected users) revealed that churn and resurrection lines were much larger than the new user line, shifting the growth team's focus from acquisition to retention and engagement.
- Eighty percent of project failures stem from people or process issues rather than strategy or execution problems. Ensure perfect execution first, because only then can you accurately evaluate whether the strategy itself is right or wrong.
- The growth team's core insight was applying a data-driven, product-driven approach to problems that companies traditionally handled through business functions like marketing, corporate social responsibility, and localization.
- Simplification is not oversimplification. It means identifying the kindergarten-level building blocks of a complex problem and layering on complexity progressively, like building a school curriculum from kindergarten through PhD level.
