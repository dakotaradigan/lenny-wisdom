# Ronny Kohavi

World expert on A/B testing and online experimentation. Former VP and Technical Fellow at Airbnb (search experience), Corporate VP at Microsoft (Experimentation Platform running 20,000+ experiments/year), and Director of Data Mining at Amazon. Author of Trustworthy Online Controlled Experiments, the definitive book on experimentation.

## Key Frameworks

### Test Everything Philosophy
Any code change or feature introduction should be in an experiment. Even small bug fixes can have surprising unexpected impact. The portfolio approach: most experiments should be incremental improvements, but allocate a portion to high-risk high-reward ideas where 80% will fail but the wins are home runs.

### The OEC (Overall Evaluation Criterion)
A single metric that captures what the experiment is trying to optimize. Designing the right OEC is critical -- it must balance short-term gains against long-term user health. Revenue gains that hurt user experience metrics are not wins. Guard rail metrics prevent false victories.

### Institutional Learning from Experiments
Run quarterly meetings on the most surprising experiments (not just winners). Surprising means the gap between expected and actual outcome is large. Document successes and failures searchably. The biggest risk is organizations that launch experiments but never summarize the learnings.

## Core Ideas

- At optimized properties like Bing search, 85-92% of experiment ideas fail to improve key metrics -- this is normal and humbling, not a sign of poor ideation
- The biggest A/B test win in Bing history was moving the ad's second line to the first line -- a trivial change worth $100M that had languished in the backlog for months because no one prioritized it
- Most gains are inch by inch: Bing's relevance team aims for 2% improvement per year from hundreds of small wins, not breakthrough moments
- Opening links in a new tab is one of the most reliably positive experiment patterns across companies (Microsoft, Airbnb, and others)
