# Hari Srinivasan

VP of Product at LinkedIn leading the Talent Solutions Product Team, LinkedIn's biggest business, including all hiring and learning products. Has been at LinkedIn for over eight years. His team drove the shift toward skills-first hiring during the COVID labor market disruption, translating people's experiences into skill sets to help rebalance supply and demand across industries.

## Key Frameworks

### Skills-First Hiring
During COVID, hospitality workers lost jobs while customer service could not hire fast enough, but the marketplace was not rebalancing because people search by job titles, not transferable skills. LinkedIn's push to translate experiences into skills helped workers discover opportunities they would never have found through title-based searching. This may represent a permanent shift in how the labor market pathways function through economic cycles.

### Connected Ecosystem Decision-Making
Everything at LinkedIn is a connected ecosystem. Decision-making is simplified because everyone knows the filter: "How does this connect people to economic opportunity?" The feed, hiring tools, learning products, and networking features all serve this single mission, which prevents teams from optimizing locally at the expense of the whole system.

## Core Ideas

- LinkedIn's feed improved dramatically by focusing on two things: helping people stay connected to their network (in-network content) and surfacing knowledge and advice from outside the network
- Building and maintaining complex systems requires keeping the whole system in view; individual feature decisions must be evaluated against the interconnected ecosystem
- The shift from cringey to interesting content on LinkedIn came from better understanding what members actually want (knowledge, relationship maintenance) and tuning algorithms toward those signals
- Product management at LinkedIn requires comfort with ambiguity across many interconnected product surfaces
- The hiring market is rebalancing through different pathways than before, with skills-based matching becoming more important than title-based matching
