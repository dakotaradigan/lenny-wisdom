# Farhan Thawar

Vice President and Head of Engineering at Shopify. Has worked for a different billionaire every decade of his career. Shopify has over 10,000 fully remote employees but continues to operate with urgency and velocity, consistently blowing away earnings expectations. Farhan is known for three things: hiring excellence, creating intensity in organizations, and choosing the hard path.

## Key Frameworks

### Choose the Hard Path
When facing multiple options, choose the harder one. If the easy path fails, you have learned nothing and worked with fewer talented people. If the hard path fails, you still win: you have done something difficult, worked with smart people, and learned something valuable. Applied to job searching: instead of sending 10 resumes a day, build an app on the company's API -- even if you do not get hired, you have learned and have something to show.

### Intensity Over Hours
Do not just work more hours -- do more per minute. Intensity is about throughput per unit of time, not total time spent. This principle drives Shopify's meeting culture: constantly cancel unnecessary meetings, use pair programming to solve problems in real-time rather than scheduling follow-ups, and bias toward synchronous problem-solving even in a remote environment.

### Delete Code Club
Shopify runs a Delete Code Club that regularly finds and removes a million-plus lines of code. Simplification through deletion creates speed, reduces maintenance burden, and forces clarity about what actually matters in the codebase.

## Core Ideas

- Looking stupid in public repeatedly is a superpower; half the time when you ask the "dumb" question, others had the same question but were afraid to ask
- The people you meet on the hard path become lifelong professional relationships because they self-select for intellectual curiosity and ambition
- Pair programming is counter-intuitive but eliminates the latency of asynchronous code review and produces better code faster
- Building resilience to rejection (from retail commission sales, cold calling, etc.) translates directly into professional fearlessness
- Great engineering leaders create intensity through cadence and structure, not through demanding overtime
