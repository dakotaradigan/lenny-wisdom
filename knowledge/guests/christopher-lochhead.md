# Christopher Lochhead

Three-time public company CMO, 13-time bestselling co-author of books including Play Bigger and Niche Down, and co-creator of the Category Pirates Substack. Known as the Godfather of Category Design with deep expertise in helping companies create and dominate new market categories rather than competing in existing ones.

## Key Frameworks

### Category Design (Frame, Name, Claim)
Companies that design new categories earn approximately 76% of the total market value in their space. Category design involves framing a new problem, naming the category with strategic languaging, and claiming leadership of that space. This is distinct from being first to ship a product -- it requires getting the world to agree with your definition of the problem, which then leads to your definition of the solution.

### Languaging
The strategic use of language to change thinking and create new perceived value. Starbucks created venti, grande, and latte to justify charging $3 for something that cost $0.10. Otis rebranded the elevator as a "vertical railway" to help people understand its purpose. The company that creates the languaging for a category wins the category.

### Backcasting vs. Forecasting
Instead of standing in the present and planning forward (forecasting), envision a radically different future five years out, make it vivid and real, then look backward to determine what steps were taken to get there. This unshackles thinking from the past and avoids incremental "better" approaches.

## Core Ideas

- The "better trap" is the most common strategic mistake: launching a better version of an existing product in an existing category almost always fails. Amazon Fire phone, Microsoft Stores, Threads, and Red Bull Cola all failed this way despite massive brand and distribution advantages.
- Product-market fit as commonly understood is backwards -- it starts from the product and tries to fit it into an existing market, reinforcing incremental thinking. Category designers start from a different future and work backward.
- Spend more time on the problem than the solution. Legendary entrepreneurs are obsessed with why a problem persists, not just with building a better product.
- Every 10x better product still fails if it competes in an existing well-understood category with a well-understood solution, because the problem makes the solution, not the other way around.
