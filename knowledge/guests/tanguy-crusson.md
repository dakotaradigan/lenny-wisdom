# Tanguy Crusson

Head of Jira Product Discovery at Atlassian, which he led from idea through internal incubation to general availability. Ten-year veteran at Atlassian with a 50/50 track record across multiple zero-to-one bets including HipChat, Stride, and Statuspage integration. Deep practitioner of building new products inside large organizations.

## Key Frameworks

### Wonder, Explore, Make, Impact
Atlassian's Point A incubation stages. Wonder validates problem and market. Explore validates solutions through prototypes and customer playback. Make builds alpha through beta with staged customer exposure. Impact launches the product and measures business results. Each gate requires a six-pager reviewed by founders and business leaders.

### Lighthouse Users Program (10 > 100 > 1,000)
A staged approach to customer exposure for new products. Start with 10 deeply understood customers who serve as proxies for the broader market. Expand to 100 to test scenario variations. Reach 1,000 to validate self-service readiness. Success criteria are qualitative in early stages, shifting to quantitative as you scale. Engineers should know lighthouse users by name to build real empathy.

### Incubate, Iterate, Integrate
Borrowed from Noah Weiss: build new experiences adjacent to but detached from the core product, iterate until they work, then integrate back into the main product. At Jira, this meant building a separate UX for product managers that did not conform to Jira's existing architecture, then folding it back in once validated.

## Core Ideas

- Failure is the most likely outcome for new bets; framing it this way protects teams from corporate over-investment and buys autonomy to hack and test without platform dependencies
- Startups have the benefit of starving; inside large companies you must artificially create scarcity and resist the urge to over-resource new bets
- Competitive myopia kills products; HipChat died partly from feature-chasing Slack instead of deepening its own user understanding
- Weekly internal comms showing velocity, customer snippets, and product demos create a perception of a high-speed train that no one wants to stand in front of
- Acquisitions should be treated like hiring, not just buying a business; the culture shock of joining a large company is the hardest part of integration
