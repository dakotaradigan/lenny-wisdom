# Ramesh Johari

Stanford professor and marketplace advisor who has spent over a decade studying how marketplaces work. Directed the data science team at oDesk (now Upwork) and has advised numerous marketplace companies including Uber, Airbnb, and Bumble. Brings an economics-first lens to marketplace product and data decisions.

## Key Frameworks

### Marketplaces Sell Friction Removal
Marketplaces do not sell the underlying goods -- hosts sell rooms, drivers sell rides. The marketplace sells the removal of transaction costs (the friction of finding, trusting, and transacting with the other side). This reframing changes everything: both sides are your customers, and your value proposition is taking friction away, not providing inventory.

### The Marketplace Readiness Litmus Test
Do you have scaled liquidity on both sides? If not, you are not yet a marketplace regardless of what you call yourself. If you have one side scaled, decide whether to grow that side further or use it to attract the other. If neither side is scaled, stop thinking about marketplace dynamics and focus on startup fundamentals. Every founder is potentially a marketplace founder -- it is a choice made after achieving scale, not an identity adopted at inception.

### Prediction vs. Decision-Making Gap
Data scientists often optimize for prediction (who will be hired? what is their LTV?) when the real business question is about decisions (how much more will they spend because of this promotion?). The differential matters enormously. Sending promotions to highest-LTV users feels safe but is often wrong -- what matters is the incremental lift, not the absolute value.

## Core Ideas

- Marketplaces are a game of whac-a-mole: most consequential changes create winners and losers, and management is about deciding whether the winners matter more than the losers to the business
- The data science cycle in marketplaces is: find potential matches, make matches, learn from matches, and feed that back -- every marketplace vertical has these three problems
- Early marketplace monetization choices can trap you later; oDesk's constant-percentage fee made sense early but led to disintermediation as relationships matured
- A marketplace business never starts as a marketplace business -- start by solving a non-marketplace friction, then evolve

## Notable Perspectives

> "A marketplace business never starts as a marketplace business, because what we think of as a marketplace business is something which at scale is removing the friction of the two sides finding each other. But when you start, you don't have that scale."
