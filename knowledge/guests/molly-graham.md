# Molly Graham

Founder of Glue Club, a community for leaders navigating rapid scale. Early employee at Google and Facebook, where she worked closely with Zuck, Sheryl Sandberg, and Chamath. Helped scale Quip (sold to Salesforce) and launch the Chan Zuckerberg Initiative.

## Key Frameworks

### Give Away Your Legos
As companies scale, your job outgrows you every few weeks. The instinct is to cling to the work you have mastered, but the path to career growth is continuously handing off your current responsibilities and taking on unfamiliar, larger challenges. The emotions of loss and territoriality are normal but not useful. At Facebook, Molly was giving away her job every three weeks.

### The J-Curve vs. Stairs
Most people plan careers as a staircase of predictable promotions every two years. The more rewarding path is jumping off cliffs: taking roles you are deeply unqualified for, enduring six to nine months of feeling like an idiot, and emerging with capabilities the staircase could never provide. Financial fear is worth listening to; fear of incompetence is a flashing green light.

### The Waterline Model
A team diagnostic borrowed from wilderness leadership. Teams are boats; problems lurk below the waterline in four layers: structural (goals, roles, expectations), dynamics (culture, decision-making), interpersonal (relationships), and intrapersonal (individual issues). The rule is "snorkel before you scuba": 80% of team problems are structural or dynamics issues, not people problems. Start by clarifying roles and expectations before blaming individuals.

### Bob the Monster
Externalize the emotional reactions that come with change (imposter syndrome, territoriality, rage) into a character named Bob. Bob's job is to make you the worst version of yourself. Your job is to let Bob's waves pass without acting on them. If an emotion lasts longer than two weeks, it is real and worth addressing. Everything shorter is just Bob.

## Core Ideas

- Eighty percent of culture at a company is defined by the personality of the founder. The operator's job is to articulate and scale what the founder naturally creates.
- No company needs more than three goals. Facebook had exactly three (growth, engagement, revenue) for five straight years. One goal must win when goals conflict. An intern who started Monday should be able to understand them.
- Firing is as important a skill as hiring. The best hirers are right about 50% of the time, which means half of all hires will need to be let go. Getting good at recognizing poor fit quickly is critical for scaling companies.
- When leading through change, your job is not to have all the answers but to get good at finding them. Saying "I don't know, let's figure it out" is a sign of strength, not weakness.
- Serve the business, not the people. When decisions get emotional or political, asking "what is right for this business?" cuts through the noise and leads to better outcomes for everyone.

## Notable Perspectives

> "You have to grow as fast as your company is growing if you really want to take advantage of the opportunity. Learning to give away what you've gotten good at and move on to the next shiny pile of Legos."

> "Winners and losers have the same goal. Goals by themselves are not enough. You have to build a process by which you follow up on the goals and hold people accountable."
