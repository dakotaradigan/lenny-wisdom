# Tom Conrad

Engineer at Apple (built spring-loaded folders in Finder), CTO of Pandora (zero to 80M users), VP of Product at Snap (right hand to Evan Spiegel), CPO at Quibi, early engineer at Pets.com. Board member at Sonos for 7+ years. Rare career spanning both billion-dollar successes and billion-dollar failures, with deep lessons from both.

## Key Frameworks

### Failures as Career Accelerants
Notable disasters open doors that quiet successes never do. Pets.com made Tom more memorable than working at an unknown e-commerce startup would have. Quibi re-ignited his passion for building software after he thought he was done. Professional satisfaction correlates poorly with external success metrics and strongly with loving the product and the people.

### The People-First Decision Model
Early in career, optimize for product passion. Quickly you learn that satisfaction correlates more with the people you collaborate with than the product itself. Trust your instinct about people during interviews -- every time Tom joined a company with misaligned values, he knew beforehand but told himself a story about why it would be fine.

### Over-Investment Arms Race
From Pets.com: when multiple overfunded competitors exist in the same market, excess capital creates irrational spending races (especially on marketing) that become unwinnable. Having too much investment can be its own albatross. Pets.com was not a stupid business -- Chewy proved the model works -- it was just too early and too overfunded.

## Core Ideas

- Not everyone needs to be a founder; the industry would be better off if more brilliant people joined existing teams rather than starting companies that go nowhere
- Timing matters enormously; Pets.com failed shipping dog food when 80% of the US was on dial-up, while Chewy built a $9B business doing the same thing a decade later
- Returning remaining capital to investors rather than burning it all is an underappreciated form of leadership; Pets.com shut down voluntarily and returned the balance
- Shortcuts in design become permanent; a placeholder animation Tom wrote at 2 AM in 1992 still exists in Apple's OS three implementations later
- Quibi's lesson: welding Silicon Valley consumer tech to Hollywood content production is fundamentally harder than either side appreciates

## Notable Perspectives

> "There's an entire category of smart, creative, hardworking, talented, borderline visionary people who would be so much better off finding a team that needs their skillset."

> "My professional satisfaction is not well correlated with external metrics and very, very correlated with do I love the thing we were building and do I love the people I was working with."
