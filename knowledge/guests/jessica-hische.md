## Key Frameworks

### Brand Refresh Scoping
First determine scope: close-in (fixing utilitarian issues like scaling, legibility, avatar versions) vs. broad (shifting vibe to attract new audience segments without alienating core users). Everything cascades from this decision. If close-in, you're talking technical fixes immediately. If broad, round one is expansive exploration that narrows each iteration.

### When to Refresh
Key signals: you're about to physically invest money in branded materials (swag, conferences, new hires), you're using a widely available font that competitors could easily copy, or your beta-stage visual identity doesn't match your product's maturity. Don't over-invest in brand at the very beginning -- wait until the product direction stabilizes.

### Cover Sets the Vibe
A brand/logo should tell people what to expect, like a book cover. It's not about driving company culture (that comes from product and team), but about generating both internal and external excitement and setting the tone for engagement.

## Core Ideas

- Most people are better at reading typography and logos than they give themselves credit for. Humans are endless absorbers of patterns. Even non-designers can sense when something is "off" -- they just can't name why. A good exercise: look at different fonts and ask what feeling each gives you.
- Don't spend venture money on massive brand exploration when you're still figuring out product direction. Pivots make early brand investments wasted money. Start with something good enough, then refresh when the business stabilizes.
- Custom typography protects you from copycats. If you use a free or widely available font, any competitor can replicate your look. Custom work creates a moat of visual identity.
- Start narratives before slides. Figure out what you're trying to say first, then design around it -- not the reverse.
