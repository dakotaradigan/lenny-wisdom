# Oji Udezue

CPO at Typeform, previously CPO at Calendly, Head of Product for communications at Atlassian, Head of Product for creation at Twitter, and product roles at Microsoft. One of the broadest careers in product management, bringing rare cross-company perspective on what works in PLG and B2B growth.

## Key Frameworks

### Workflow Frequency-Breadth Matrix
Plot B2B opportunities on two axes: how many departments use the workflow (everyone vs niche) and how frequently it is performed (daily vs monthly). High-frequency everyone workflows are dominated by Microsoft/Google. The sweet spot for B2B SaaS is high-frequency niche (High Ni) -- workflows a specific department does daily. Strategy is navigating from lower quadrants toward higher ones.

### Zone of Benefit (3X Threshold)
Products must be at least 3X better than the status quo for people to notice and be willing to switch. A 20% improvement is imperceptible. This helps identify your true ICP: the people for whom your product delivers a 3X or greater improvement are your best, least price-sensitive customers.

### Organic vs Synthetic Virality
Products that try to manufacture virality through growth hacks but have a weak core experience will fail. Slack was not even engineered to be viral -- you could not discover other teams using it in your building. It spread because people went to lunch and could not stop talking about it. The bedrock of virality is solving a sharp problem exceptionally well.

## Core Ideas

- Customer science is not the solution to everything; OpenAI would never have built what they built through customer development alone -- innovation and problem space selection still drive success
- Twitter's ICP is bifurcated: the most accomplished people in the world who have something to say, plus the 90% who follow and form communities around them; Elon's changes suggest misunderstanding this
- Frameworks are dangerous when applied blindly; you must understand the underlying empirical relationships to adapt them to your stage and context
- Enterprise software will trend toward consumer-like product management over the next decade, which is the broader meaning of PLG
