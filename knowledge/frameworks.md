# Frameworks Index

55 named product frameworks from Lenny's podcast and newsletter, organized by category. Each framework includes its source, description, key steps, and when to use it.

---

## Growth

### North Star Metric
**Source:** Lenny Rachitsky (newsletter) / Hila Qu (Reforge, GitLab)
A single key metric that best captures the core value your product delivers to customers. Used to align teams and drive growth decisions. Choose by identifying the metric that reflects customer value received, not revenue.
**When to use:** When your team lacks alignment on what to optimize, or when you're drowning in metrics and need one to rule them all.

### Racecar Growth Framework
**Source:** Dan Hockenmaier & Lenny Rachitsky
Analogizes growth strategy to a racecar: the **engine** (growth loops), **turbo boosts** (one-time accelerants like PR or launches), **lubricants** (optimizations like conversion rate), and **fuel** (capital investment). Think about growth holistically rather than as isolated tactics.
**When to use:** When building or auditing your overall growth strategy. Forces you to identify which component is the bottleneck.

### Adjacent User Theory
**Source:** Bangaly Kaba (Instagram, Facebook, YouTube)
Growth comes from understanding and serving users just outside your current user base. These "adjacent users" are aware of your product but face barriers to engagement. Map who they are, what blocks them, and systematically remove those barriers.
**When to use:** When core user growth plateaus but awareness is high. The next wave of growth comes from adjacent users, not more of the same.

### Growth Accounting (CURR Framework)
**Source:** Lenny's Newsletter / Duolingo case study
Decompose user growth into: Current User Retention Rate (CURR), new users, resurrected users, and churned users. Duolingo used this to identify CURR as their most leveraged metric, driving a massive growth inflection.
**When to use:** When diagnosing where growth is stalling. Separates retention, acquisition, and resurrection into distinct levers.

### S-Curve Stacking
**Source:** Elena Verna (Miro, Amplitude, Dropbox)
Every growth lever follows an S-curve that eventually plateaus. Proactively identify and layer new growth models (product-led, sales-led, viral) before the current one flattens. Start building the next S-curve when the current one is at ~70% of its potential.
**When to use:** When your primary growth channel is maturing. Plan 18 months ahead for the next growth engine.

### The Law of Shitty Clickthroughs
**Source:** Andrew Chen (a16z)
Over time, every marketing channel's effectiveness degrades as it becomes saturated. Early movers get outsized returns; late adopters see diminishing returns. Plan for channel decay and always be testing new channels.
**When to use:** When a once-reliable channel starts underperforming. It's not broken — it's mature.

### Five-Step Startup Growth Diagnostic
**Source:** Jason Cohen (WP Engine)
When growth stalls, examine five areas in order: (1) Logo retention — are customers staying? (2) Pricing — are you capturing value? (3) Net revenue retention — are existing customers expanding? (4) Marketing channels — are you reaching the right people? (5) Target market — are you in the right market? Dig past surface explanations.
**When to use:** When SaaS growth stalls and you need a systematic diagnostic rather than guessing.

### Hook Model
**Source:** Nir Eyal (author of Hooked)
Products become habit-forming through a four-phase loop: **Trigger** (external or internal cue), **Action** (simplest behavior in anticipation of reward), **Variable Reward** (satisfies the user's need while leaving them wanting more), **Investment** (user puts something in that improves the product). Each cycle strengthens the habit.
**When to use:** When designing for engagement and retention in consumer products. Especially useful for products that need habitual use.

### Hierarchy of Engagement
**Source:** Sarah Tavel (Benchmark, Pinterest)
Three levels for consumer products: (1) **Growing engaged users** — find your core action and get users to complete it, (2) **Retaining users** — create accruing benefits that make the product harder to leave, (3) **Self-perpetuating** — user actions create virtuous loops that bring in more users. Build each level in order.
**When to use:** When designing retention and engagement strategy for consumer products.

### Hierarchy of Marketplaces
**Source:** Sarah Tavel (Benchmark, Pinterest)
Three phases: (1) **Focus** — find and measure "happy GMV" (transactions where both sides are delighted), (2) **Tip** — reach the tipping point where the marketplace becomes self-sustaining, (3) **Dominate** — build network effects that make it impossible for competitors to catch up.
**When to use:** When building or scaling a marketplace business.

### Value Delivery Engine
**Source:** Sean Ellis (Dropbox, LogMeIn)
Map the journey from first touch to "must-have" experience. Identify where users drop off and focus relentlessly on getting more users to the "aha moment." Activation is the highest-leverage growth investment.
**When to use:** When activation rates are low or you need to find your product's aha moment.

### Forget the Funnel (Customer-Led Growth)
**Source:** Gia Laudi (Forget the Funnel)
Replace traditional marketing funnels with customer-research-driven approach: deeply understand customer jobs-to-be-done through switch interviews, then align marketing, product, and sales to those jobs.
**When to use:** When traditional funnel optimization isn't working. Shifts focus from stages to customer motivations.

## Strategy

### DHM Model (Delight, Hard-to-Copy, Margin-Enhancing)
**Source:** Gibson Biddle (Netflix, Chegg)
Test whether initiatives are **Delightful** for customers, **Hard-to-copy** by competitors, and **Margin-enhancing** for the business. All three must be true for a winning strategy. Netflix's recommendation engine passed all three; its original content strategy was designed around this model.
**When to use:** When evaluating strategic initiatives or building your product roadmap. Filters out ideas that are delightful but easy to copy.

### GEM Prioritization
**Source:** Gibson Biddle (Netflix, Chegg)
Prioritize roadmap items by evaluating **Growth** potential, **Engagement** impact, and **Monetization** opportunity. Score each initiative on all three dimensions and stack-rank.
**When to use:** When you have too many ideas and need a structured way to prioritize your roadmap.

### Strategic Narrative (Five Steps)
**Source:** Andy Raskin
Five steps: (1) Name a shift in the world, (2) Name the stakes (winners and losers), (3) Name the new game's object, (4) Show obstacles, (5) Show how you overcome them. Replaces the "arrogant doctor" pitch with a story about the world changing.
**When to use:** When crafting your company narrative, investor pitch, or sales deck.

### Positioning Framework (Five Components)
**Source:** April Dunford
Define positioning through: (1) Competitive alternatives, (2) Unique attributes, (3) Differentiated value, (4) Target customer segments, (5) Market category. Start from competitive alternatives, not from your own features.
**When to use:** When your product is misunderstood, when entering a new market, or when competitive landscape shifts.

### Jobs to Be Done (JTBD)
**Source:** Bob Moesta / multiple episodes
Users "hire" your product to do a job for them. Understanding the job (not features) reveals your real competitive set and value proposition. Focus on the struggling moment, the push/pull forces, and the hiring/firing criteria.
**When to use:** When you need to understand why customers switch to (or away from) your product.

### Amazon PR/FAQ (Working Backwards)
**Source:** Bill Carr / Jason Shah
Write a mock press release and FAQ for the product before building it. Forces clarity on customer value, differentiation, and the "so what" before any code is written.
**When to use:** Before starting any significant new product or feature. Forces customer-centric thinking.

### 11-Star Experience
**Source:** Brian Chesky (Airbnb)
Imagine your product experience on a scale of 1-10, then push to imagine what 11 stars would look like. Work backwards from the ideal to find the practically achievable but still transformative experience.
**When to use:** When your product feels incremental and you need to think bigger about the customer experience.

### Product Strategy Playbook (Five Phases)
**Source:** Chandra Janakiraman (Meta, Headspace, VRChat)
Eight-to-twelve week strategy process: (1) Preparation — 4 weeks of data gathering, (2) Strategy sprint, (3) Design sprint, (4) Document writing, (5) Rollout. Strategy = strategic pillars + explicit non-focuses + the "why."
**When to use:** When you need to develop or refresh product strategy for a team or business unit.

### Pattern Breakers (Inflections, Insights, Founder-Future Fit)
**Source:** Mike Maples Jr (Floodgate)
Breakthrough startups require three elements: (1) An **inflection** (technology or behavior shift), (2) A **non-consensus insight** about that inflection, (3) **Founder-future fit** (why this founder for this future). Most startup ideas fail because they're consensus views riding no real inflection.
**When to use:** When evaluating startup ideas or pivoting. Tests whether your idea has the raw material for breakthrough success.

### Seven Powers
**Source:** Hamilton Helmer
Seven durable strategic advantages: Scale Economies, Network Economies, Counter-Positioning, Switching Costs, Branding, Cornered Resource, Process Power. A business must achieve at least one to sustain profitability.
**When to use:** When evaluating long-term defensibility of a business or product strategy.

## Prioritization

### LNO Framework
**Source:** Shreyas Doshi (Stripe, Google, Twitter)
Categorize all PM tasks as **Leverage** (10x+ return), **Neutral** (1:1 return), or **Overhead** (low return). Apply perfectionism only to L tasks, do N tasks adequately, and minimize effort on O tasks. The same task can be L, N, or O depending on context.
**When to use:** When overwhelmed with tasks and need to ruthlessly prioritize your time.

### RICE Prioritization
**Source:** Sean Ellis / multiple episodes
Score features by **Reach** (how many users affected), **Impact** (how much per user), **Confidence** (how sure you are), and **Effort** (how much work). Multiply for a comparable score. Helps teams compare disparate opportunities objectively.
**When to use:** When you need a lightweight, data-informed way to compare feature ideas.

### ICE Scoring
**Source:** Sean Ellis
Prioritize experiments by rating **Impact**, **Confidence**, and **Ease** on a 1-10 scale; multiply for a quick comparable score. Lighter-weight than RICE, designed for experiment prioritization.
**When to use:** When running growth experiments and need to decide which to run next.

### Opportunity Solution Tree
**Source:** Teresa Torres
Map desired outcomes at the top, branch into opportunity spaces, then solutions and experiments as leaves. Ensures solutions connect to real opportunities, not just ideas. Visualizes the discovery process.
**When to use:** When your team generates lots of ideas but struggles to connect them to real user problems.

### Opportunity Cost over ROI
**Source:** Shreyas Doshi
Stop chasing positive ROI (which biases toward quick wins) and instead minimize opportunity cost. ROI thinking shrinks the denominator, favoring low-hanging fruit. Opportunity cost thinking asks "is this the best use of my time?" rather than "is this a good use of my time?"
**When to use:** When your team defaults to safe, incremental work and never tackles the big strategic bets.

## Product-Market Fit

### Sean Ellis PMF Test (40% Rule)
**Source:** Sean Ellis / Rahul Vohra (Superhuman)
Survey users: "How would you feel if you could no longer use this product?" If 40%+ say "very disappointed," you have product-market fit. More predictive and actionable than NPS.
**When to use:** When you need to measure whether you've achieved PMF, or when tracking progress toward it.

### Superhuman PMF Engine
**Source:** Rahul Vohra (Superhuman)
Operationalize the Sean Ellis test: (1) Segment users, (2) Identify what "very disappointed" users love, (3) Understand what "somewhat disappointed" users want, (4) Build a roadmap to systematically increase the PMF score. Superhuman used this to go from 22% to 58%.
**When to use:** When you're pre-PMF and need a systematic process to get there, not just a measurement.

## Leadership & Management

### Radical Candor
**Source:** Kim Scott
Effective feedback requires both **challenging directly** and **caring personally**. The intersection of these two dimensions creates the most productive working relationships. Without caring, it's obnoxious aggression; without challenging, it's ruinous empathy.
**When to use:** When giving feedback, coaching reports, or building a feedback culture on your team.

### Give Away Your Legos
**Source:** Molly Graham (Facebook, Google)
As your company scales, deliberately hand off responsibilities you've built ("your Legos") to new hires so you can take on bigger challenges. The emotional resistance is natural but must be overcome. The people who can't give away their Legos become the bottleneck.
**When to use:** When scaling a team and struggling to delegate, or when you notice a leader hoarding responsibilities.

### The Magic Loop (Five Steps)
**Source:** Ethan Evans (Amazon)
Five-step career advancement process: (1) Ask your manager what's most important, (2) Do that work, (3) Ask for feedback, (4) Repeat, (5) Eventually ask what you need to do to get promoted. Works even with difficult managers because it aligns your work with their priorities.
**When to use:** When coaching reports on career growth, or when you feel stuck in your own career.

### Energy Audit / Zone of Genius
**Source:** Matt Mochary (CEO coach)
Audit every activity in your day for whether it gives or drains energy. Systematically eliminate draining activities by delegating, automating, or dropping them. Naturally gravitate toward your zone of genius — work that is uniquely excellent and energizing for you.
**When to use:** When feeling burned out, or when coaching leaders to focus on their highest-impact work.

### Working With Me / Self-User Manual
**Source:** Claire Hughes Johnson (Stripe)
Write a document describing how you work: communication preferences, values, quirks, what makes you stressed, how you make decisions. Share with your team to accelerate trust and reduce friction.
**When to use:** When joining a new team, starting a new role, or when team dynamics need improvement.

### 15% Rule
**Source:** Carole Robin (Stanford "Touchy Feely")
Push yourself 15% beyond your comfort zone in interpersonal interactions — enough to grow but not so far that you shut down. Builds relationship skills incrementally.
**When to use:** When developing interpersonal skills or coaching someone to have more candid conversations.

## Decision-Making

### Pre-mortem
**Source:** Shreyas Doshi / Annie Duke
Before launching, imagine the project has failed and work backwards to identify what went wrong. Set kill criteria in advance to commit to specific actions if warning signals appear. Creates psychological safety for raising risks.
**When to use:** Before any major launch, investment, or commitment. Especially valuable when the team is overly optimistic.

### One-Way vs. Two-Way Door Decisions
**Source:** Amazon/Bezos (referenced by multiple guests)
Classify decisions as irreversible (**one-way doors**, require careful deliberation) or reversible (**two-way doors**, should be made quickly). Most decisions are two-way doors and should not be over-analyzed.
**When to use:** When your team is slow to make decisions. Helps calibrate the right amount of analysis per decision.

### Disagree and Commit
**Source:** Amazon (referenced by Ethan Evans, Tamar Yehoshua)
Have backbone and voice disagreement during the decision process, but once a decision is made, fully commit to executing it regardless of your personal position.
**When to use:** When a team is paralyzed by disagreement, or when you need to move forward despite unresolved debate.

### Fear Setting
**Source:** Tim Ferriss (via Paul Millerd episode)
Write down fears about a decision, how to mitigate them, and the cost of inaction. Reframes risk by exposing that inaction is often more costly than the feared action.
**When to use:** When fear is blocking a career or product decision. Makes the implicit explicit.

### Five Whys
**Source:** Yuhki Yamashita (Figma) / multiple episodes
Ask "why" repeatedly (at least five times) to drill past surface symptoms to root causes. Used in postmortems, churn analysis, and product debugging.
**When to use:** When debugging any problem — product, process, or organizational.

## Communication

### Concentric Circles (Comms Strategy)
**Source:** Lulu Cheng Meservey (Substack)
Spread ideas by targeting concentric audience rings: start with power users at the core, then expand outward. Each ring amplifies the message to the next, creating organic reach.
**When to use:** When launching a product, feature, or narrative and need to build organic momentum.

### Cultural Erogenous Zones
**Source:** Lulu Cheng Meservey (Substack)
Find what your audience already deeply cares about and shape your message to fit their existing passions and worldview, rather than trying to change what they care about.
**When to use:** When crafting PR, marketing, or positioning messages. Meets the audience where they already are.

### Pressure = Force / Area
**Source:** Lulu Cheng Meservey (Substack)
Borrowed from physics: decrease your target surface area and focus your message to a sharp point to achieve maximum communications impact with limited resources. Especially useful for startups.
**When to use:** When you have limited marketing budget and need maximum impact.

### What, So What, Now What
**Source:** Matt Abrahams (Stanford)
Structure for spontaneous communication: state the observation (**What**), explain why it matters (**So What**), then suggest next steps (**Now What**). Creates clear, actionable communication on the fly.
**When to use:** When giving impromptu feedback, status updates, or any unstructured communication moment.

### PREP (Point, Reason, Example, Point)
**Source:** Matt Abrahams (Stanford)
For making a point on the spot: state your **Point**, give a **Reason**, provide an **Example**, then restate the **Point**. Creates clear, memorable spontaneous communication.
**When to use:** When you need to articulate a position quickly in a meeting or conversation.

## Pricing & Monetization

### Willingness to Pay Conversations
**Source:** Madhavan Ramanujam (Simon-Kucher)
Have pricing conversations with customers before building the product. Use: acceptable/expensive/prohibitively expensive price questions, purchase probability scales, most/least important feature exercises, and trade-off scenarios. 20% of features drive 80% of willingness to pay.
**When to use:** Before setting prices on anything new. Prevents the #1 pricing mistake: guessing.

### Leaders, Fillers, and Killers
**Source:** Madhavan Ramanujam (Simon-Kucher)
Framework for packaging: **Leaders** are must-have features driving purchase decisions, **Fillers** add perceived value at low cost, **Killers** would destroy the package for most but can be sold as add-ons. Use most/least exercises to categorize features.
**When to use:** When designing pricing tiers, bundles, or packaging for any product.

### Subscription Value Loop
**Source:** Lenny's Newsletter
Framework for growing consumer subscription businesses. Focuses on the virtuous cycle between delivering value, retaining subscribers, and reinvesting revenue into more value creation.
**When to use:** When building or optimizing a subscription-based product.

## Execution & Process

### OKRs
**Source:** Christina Wodtke (Stanford, Radical Focus)
Objectives and Key Results: set ambitious qualitative objectives with measurable key results. Maintain a weekly cadence of check-ins. Distinguish committed vs. aspirational OKRs. The objective inspires; the key results prove you achieved it.
**When to use:** When setting team or company goals. Most effective with a weekly rhythm.

### Shape Up
**Source:** Ryan Singer (Basecamp)
Plan, build, and ship in fixed 6-week cycles with 2-week cooldowns. Pitch shaped work (not fully specified, not too vague), give teams autonomy to execute, and use the appetite (time budget) as a constraint rather than estimating.
**When to use:** When sprint-based agile feels too micro-managed or too uncertain.

### Linear Method
**Source:** Karri Saarinen (Linear)
Build opinionated software by working in consistent cycles (not sprints), prioritizing craft and quality, and building the product the team itself wants to use. Cycles run on automated schedules.
**When to use:** When your team values craft and wants a calmer, more predictable development cadence.

### DORA Metrics
**Source:** Nicole Forsgren (Google, DORA)
Measure engineering team performance via four metrics: **deployment frequency**, **lead time for changes**, **change failure rate**, and **time to restore service**. Used for diagnosis and benchmarking, not punishment.
**When to use:** When assessing engineering team health or tracking improvement over time.

### Impact-First Product Management
**Source:** Matt LeMay
Three steps: (1) Start with the business goal (not team OKRs), (2) Ruthlessly prioritize by impact on that goal, (3) Measure your actual impact. Avoids "OKR theater" where teams optimize for their own metrics instead of business outcomes.
**When to use:** When teams are busy but impact is unclear, or when OKRs feel disconnected from business results.

## Productivity & Focus

### Indistractable (Four Steps)
**Source:** Nir Eyal
Four steps to overcome distraction: (1) Master internal triggers — understand the discomfort driving the distraction, (2) Make time for traction — schedule your values, (3) Hack back external triggers — eliminate unnecessary notifications, (4) Prevent distraction with pacts — create commitments.
**When to use:** When struggling with focus, or when coaching a team on deep work habits.

### 10-Minute Rule
**Source:** Nir Eyal
When tempted by distraction, tell yourself you can give in — in 10 minutes. The urge almost always passes. Works because it removes the feeling of deprivation while leveraging the natural decay of impulses.
**When to use:** When you need a simple, practical technique for managing in-the-moment distractions.

### Timeboxing over To-Do Lists
**Source:** Nir Eyal
Replace to-do lists with a timeboxed calendar. Assign every hour of your day to a specific activity. The output is not a checked-off list but a fully lived schedule. To-do lists are infinite; timeboxes are finite.
**When to use:** When your to-do list keeps growing and you never feel "done."
